# Family Commandments Project — Facilitator Guide

## Overview
This is a collaborative family project where each person contributes ideas for your family's shared values, rules, and mottos. The final product is a poster or artwork displayed in your home — a living reminder of what your family stands for.

## Materials Needed
- Printed worksheets (age-appropriate version for each child)
- Pencils, crayons, markers
- Sticky notes or index cards (for the combining session)
- Large poster board or frame-worthy paper (for the final artifact)

---

## The Process (3 Sessions)

### Session 1: Individual Brainstorming (20–40 min)
**Goal:** Each person fills out their worksheet independently.

**Tips for facilitating:**
- Do your own worksheet too — kids take it more seriously when parents participate
- For the youngest kids (6–8), sit with them and *scribe* their ideas if writing is a barrier. Ask the prompts out loud and write what they say
- For middle kids (9–11), let them work independently but check in after 10 minutes
- For older kids (12+), give them space — they may want privacy to think
- **Don't correct or judge ideas during this phase.** "No TV before noon" is just as valid as "Be kind to strangers"
- If a kid is stuck, try: *"Think about a time you were really happy at home. What was happening?"* or *"What's something that bugs you that you wish we did differently?"*
- It's okay if they only come up with 3–4 ideas. Quality over quantity.

### Session 2: Share & Combine (30–45 min, family together)
**Goal:** Everyone shares their ideas. The family groups similar ones and begins drafting the final list.

**How to run it:**
1. Go around the table. Each person shares ONE idea at a time (round-robin keeps it fair)
2. Write each idea on a sticky note or index card
3. After all ideas are shared, spread them out on a table
4. Together, group similar ideas (e.g., "clean up after yourself" and "ABC — Always Be Clean" are the same spirit)
5. For each group, pick the best wording — or invent new wording together
6. Vote or discuss: does everyone agree this belongs? Aim for consensus, not majority rule
7. Target 7–12 final commandments (doesn't have to be exactly 10)

**Facilitation notes:**
- If an older kid dismisses a younger kid's idea, redirect: *"I hear you, but let's figure out what [name] is really getting at. What's the feeling behind it?"*
- If someone's idea doesn't make the list, acknowledge it: *"That's a great thought. It might be more of a personal goal than a family rule — and that's okay too"*
- Some ideas are rules ("Fix or toss broken things"), some are mottos ("See a need, fill a need"), some are values ("Playing is important"). All forms are welcome.

### Session 3: Create the Artifact (45–60 min)
**Goal:** Turn the final list into something display-worthy.

**Options for the final piece:**
- **Hand-lettered poster** — each person writes or decorates one commandment
- **Illustrated poster** — each commandment gets a small drawing next to it
- **Printed design** — use Canva, Word, or similar to create a polished print (older kids can lead this)
- **Mixed media** — combine handwriting, drawings, photos, stickers
- **Framed print** — design digitally, print at a local shop, frame it

**Tips:**
- Let each kid "own" at least one commandment on the final poster (they write it, draw it, or choose its color)
- Add "The [Last Name] Family Commandments" and the date at the top
- Consider making it a yearly tradition — revisit and update each year

## Dealing with Common Challenges

| Challenge | Try This |
|---|---|
| Kid says "I don't know" | Use the scenario prompts on their worksheet — concrete situations unlock abstract thinking |
| Kid copies a sibling's ideas | That's fine! It means they agree. Ask: "What would YOU add that's different?" |
| Ideas are silly or unrealistic | Write them down anyway. During Session 2, the group naturally filters. Don't be the filter yourself. |
| One kid dominates discussion | Use the round-robin format strictly. "Hold that thought — it's [name]'s turn" |
| Disagreement on final list | If it's contentious, table it: "Let's come back to that one. What do we ALL agree on?" |
| Perfectionism on the poster | Remind them: imperfect and done > perfect and never finished. The meaning matters more than the art. |

## Example Commandments (for YOUR reference only — don't show kids before they brainstorm)

These are just to give you a sense of the range. Don't seed their thinking with these.

- "See a need, fill a need"
- "ABC — Always Be Clean"
- "Fix it or toss it"
- "Playing is important"
- "Take care of our bodies"
- "Say what you mean, mean what you say"
- "If you open it, close it. If you use it, put it back."
- "Ask for help before you get frustrated"
- "Celebrate each other's wins"
- "Be honest, even when it's hard"
- "We don't quit — we adjust"
- "Leave it better than you found it"
- "Do dangerous things carefully"
- "Boring chores done fast = more fun time"
