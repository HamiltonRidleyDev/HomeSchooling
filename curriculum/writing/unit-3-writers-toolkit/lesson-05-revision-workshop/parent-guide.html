Lesson 5: Revision Workshop

Unit 3: Writer's Toolkit — Parent Guide

Duration: 35 minutes
Materials: This guide, Revision in Action worksheet, a piece of earlier writing from each child
Ages: All tiers (6–15)
Curriculum: Writing

Why This Lesson Matters

This is the most important skill lesson in the entire curriculum. Revision is where writing becomes good. Not drafting—revising. The difference between a mediocre writer and a skilled one is almost never raw talent. It is the willingness to go back and make it better.

Professional writers revise 5–10 times. Hemingway rewrote the ending of A Farewell to Arms 47 times. Roald Dahl said, “By the time I am nearing the end of a story, the first part will have been reread and altered and corrected at least one hundred and fifty times.”

Mindset Tip

Revision is not punishment. It is not “fixing mistakes.” It is craft. It is the sculptor chipping away marble to reveal the statue inside. Frame it this way from the very beginning and your kids will not dread it—they will feel empowered by it. The message: “Your first draft captured the idea. Revision makes the writing match how good that idea deserves to be.”

Lesson Flow

Step 1 — The Myth of the Perfect First Draft (5 min)

Read this passage aloud—it is an early draft of the opening of Charlie and the Chocolate Factory by Roald Dahl:

Early Draft

“There was a boy called Charlie Bucket who lived with his family in a small house. They were very poor. They didn't have enough food. Charlie wanted chocolate but couldn't have any. There was a chocolate factory near their house.”

Published Version

“These two very old people are the father and mother of Mr. Bucket. Their names are Grandpa Joe and Grandma Josephine. And these two very old people are the father and mother of Mrs. Bucket. Their names are Grandpa George and Grandma Georgina. This is Mr. Bucket. And this is Mrs. Bucket. Mr. and Mrs. Bucket have a small boy whose name is Charlie.”

Ask your kids: “Which one sounds like a real book? What's different?” The early draft tells facts. The published version introduces characters with personality and rhythm. That transformation happened through revision—not in one magical sitting.

The takeaway: Even Roald Dahl wrote a boring first draft. Every great writer does. The first draft is just you telling yourself the story. Revision is where you tell it to the reader.

Step 2 — The 5 Revision Techniques (10 min)

Walk through each technique using the “before” paragraph below as a live example. Apply each technique one at a time so kids see the paragraph transform step by step.

Live Example — Before: “We went to the beach. It was really hot. The water was cold. We played in the sand. I made a sandcastle. It was big. My brother knocked it down. I was mad. Then we got ice cream. It was good. We went home. It was a nice day.”
1. Read Aloud

The ear catches what the eye misses. Read the paragraph out loud. Immediately you hear the choppy, repetitive rhythm: “It was... It was... It was...” Mark anything that sounds awkward, repetitive, or flat when spoken.

2. The 20% Cut

Every piece of writing can lose 20% of its words and get better. Count the words (the example has ~55). Target: ~44 words. Look for: unnecessary words (“really,” “then”), sentences that say nothing new (“It was a nice day”), repeated ideas. Cut ruthlessly.

3. Strong Verb Hunt

Replace weak verbs + adverbs with one strong verb. “We went to the beach” → “We raced to the beach.” “My brother knocked it down” → “My brother stomped through it.” One strong verb does the work of a weak verb plus an adverb.

4. First Sentence Scan

Read only the first sentence of each paragraph. Do they tell the story alone? If you read just the openers and can follow the story, the structure is solid. If they all sound the same (“We did this. We did that.”), you need variety.

5. The “So What?” Test

After each sentence or paragraph, ask: “So what?” If a sentence doesn't move the story forward, add emotion, or give necessary detail—it doesn't earn its place. “We went home”—so what? Cut it or make it matter: “We trudged to the car, sunburned and sandy, already planning tomorrow's castle.”

Live Example — After (revised together): “We raced to the beach, the sand scorching our bare feet. I crouched at the shoreline and sculpted a castle with turrets and a moat, packing each wall tight. My brother charged through it with a war cry, scattering towers across the tide line. I wanted to scream—but then Mom appeared with two dripping cones of mint chip, and the castle didn't matter anymore.”

Read the before and after side by side. Ask: “Same day. Same events. Which one makes you FEEL like you were there?”

Step 3 — Live Revision: Your Own Work (15 min)

Each child pulls out a piece of writing from an earlier lesson (Lesson 1 sentence work, Lesson 3 hooks, Lesson 4 show-don't-tell, etc.). Together, apply all 5 techniques. Walk around and help each child with at least one technique. Let them work through the rest independently.

Use the Revision in Action worksheet to guide this process step by step.

Step 4 — Share Before & After (5 min)

Each child reads their favorite sentence BEFORE revision, then the AFTER version. The family applauds the improvement. This is important—it makes revision feel like a victory, not a chore.

Making Revision Feel Empowering
  • Celebrate cuts. “You cut 30 words and it got BETTER! That takes guts.”
  • Use sports/art analogies. Athletes watch film and fix their form. Musicians practice passages over and over. Writers revise.
  • Never say “fix it.” Say “make it stronger,” “level it up,” or “polish it.”
  • Show your own bad drafts. If you write emails, texts, anything—show them a message you rewrote before sending. Revision is everywhere.
  • Keep both versions. Seeing the before and after side by side is the most powerful motivator. It proves the work was worth it.

Age-Tier Notes

Primary (K–1, age 6): Does a simplified version on the worksheet—find 3 “tired” words in a short paragraph and replace them with “exciting” words from a word bank. Focus on the idea that we can always make our writing “more exciting.” Read aloud together and celebrate changes.
Elementary (3rd–4th, ages 9–10): Completes the provided practice paragraph revision on the worksheet, plus applies 2 techniques (read aloud + strong verb hunt) to their own earlier writing. The 20% cut can be introduced but don't stress exact word counts.
Middle School (5th–7th, ages 11–13): Full exercise—revise the practice paragraph, then apply all 5 techniques to their own work. Encourage them to track word count before and after. Should produce a clear before/after comparison.
High School (9th+, age 15): Full exercise with higher expectations. After revising their own piece, write a short reflection (3–5 sentences) on which technique made the biggest difference and why. This builds metacognitive awareness of their own revision process.

What Success Looks Like

  • Every child can name at least 2 revision techniques from memory
  • Every child has a before/after comparison they feel proud of
  • The room feels energized, not defeated—revision felt like leveling up, not getting corrected
  • Kids begin to internalize: the first draft is just the beginning
