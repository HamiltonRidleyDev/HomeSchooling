Section 1: Study the Shift

All Tiers

Read these three versions of the same message: “We should get a dog.” Notice what changes.

Audience 1 — To a Parent

“I've been researching dog breeds that are good with kids. A Labrador retriever is gentle, trainable, and the vet costs average $500 per year. I'm willing to walk it every morning before school and save my allowance for food. Here's my plan for how we'd handle it as a family…”

TONE: Persuasive, respectful, logical  |  STRATEGY: Facts, responsibility, a plan  |  FORMALITY: Medium — polite but still personal
Audience 2 — To a Best Friend

“DUDE we might get a dog!!! I'm literally begging my parents lol. I want a golden retriever SO BAD. Can you imagine?? We could bring it to the park!!”

TONE: Casual, excited, emotional  |  STRATEGY: Shared excitement, fun  |  FORMALITY: Very low — slang, caps, exclamation marks
Audience 3 — To a Landlord

“Dear Mr. Johnson, I am writing to request permission to keep a pet in our apartment. Per the lease agreement, I understand there may be an additional pet deposit. I would be happy to provide veterinary records and references upon request.”

TONE: Formal, professional, respectful  |  STRATEGY: References rules, offers documentation  |  FORMALITY: High — no contractions, business language
Key Insight

Same message. Same person writing. Completely different writing. That is audience awareness — knowing that who is reading changes how you write.

Section 2: The Shapeshifter Challenge

Elementary, Middle School, and High School

Pick a message (from this list or make up your own):

  • “I want to change my bedtime.”
  • “This product/game/movie is terrible.”
  • “We should take a family vacation to ___________.”
  • “I think [school subject] should be taught differently.”
  • Your own: ________________________________________________

My message: ________________________________________________

Audience 1: Your Parent or Guardian

Persuasive, respectful, logical. Include reasons and evidence.

 

Audience 2: Your Best Friend

Casual, honest, fun. Write how you actually talk.

 

Audience 3: A Teacher, Principal, or Official

Formal, professional, well-organized. Proper greeting and closing.

 

Section 3: Analyze the Shift

Middle School and High School

Look at your three versions. What changed?

Vocabulary

What words did you use for the friend version that you would NOT use for the teacher version?

 
 

Tone

How did the feeling change between versions? Describe the tone of each.

 
 

Details

What did you include in one version that you left out of another? Why?

 
 

Length

Which version was longest? _________________ Why do you think that is?

 
High School Extension

Reread all three versions. What stayed the same across all of them? That consistency is your voice—the part of your writing that is always, recognizably you, no matter who you are writing for.

 
 

Section 4: Find Your Voice

All Tiers (Elementary and Up)

Your VOICE is how you naturally sound on paper—your personality, your rhythm, your word choices. It is what makes your writing sound like YOU and not like anyone else.

Free Write (5 minutes)

Write about something you care deeply about. No rules. No audience. Just write like you are explaining it to someone you trust. Go.

 

Discover Your Voice

Now read it back. Underline 2–3 sentences that sound MOST like you.

What makes it sound like me?

 
 
Remember

Adapting your writing for different audiences is not being fake. It is being a skilled communicator. You talk differently to your grandmother than to your best friend—but you are still you in both conversations. Your voice stays. The style shifts. That is not dishonesty. That is writing.
