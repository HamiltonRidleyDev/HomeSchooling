Lesson 6: Audience & Voice

Unit 3: Writer's Toolkit — Parent Guide

Duration: 35 minutes
Materials: This guide, The Shapeshifter worksheet
Ages: All tiers (6–15)
Curriculum: Writing

Lesson Overview

The key insight of this lesson: writing is not one thing—it shapeshifts based on who is reading. The same person who texts “lol omw” to a friend writes “Dear Mr. Thompson, Thank you for your time” in an email. They are not being fake—they are being a skilled communicator. This lesson teaches kids to do that consciously.

The second half introduces voice—the writer's personality on the page. Audience awareness is about adapting to the reader. Voice is about knowing who YOU are as a writer. Both matter.

Lesson Flow

Step 1 — Hook: Same Message, Three Ways (5 min)

Read these three versions aloud. Do NOT tell your kids the topic first. Just read all three and ask: “Which one sounds right?”

Mentor Examples — “We Should Get a Dog”

Version 1 — To a Parent (Persuasive, Logical)

“I've been researching dog breeds that are good with kids. A Labrador retriever is gentle, trainable, and the vet costs average $500 per year. I'm willing to walk it every morning before school and save my allowance for food. Here's my plan for how we'd handle it as a family…”

Version 2 — To a Best Friend (Casual, Excited)

“DUDE we might get a dog!!! I'm literally begging my parents lol. I want a golden retriever SO BAD. Can you imagine?? We could bring it to the park!!”

Version 3 — To a Landlord (Formal, Professional)

“Dear Mr. Johnson, I am writing to request permission to keep a pet in our apartment. Per the lease agreement, I understand there may be an additional pet deposit. I would be happy to provide veterinary records and references upon request.”

After reading all three, reveal the answer: “They ALL sound right—for their audience. Same core message. Completely different writing. THAT is audience awareness.”

Step 2 — Discuss: What Changes? (5 min)

Ask the kids to identify what shifted between the three versions. Guide toward these five categories:

  1. Vocabulary — formal words vs. slang vs. technical terms
  2. Tone — serious/playful, emotional/logical, formal/casual
  3. Detail level — what you include and what you leave out
  4. Formality — contractions, sentence length, greetings
  5. Purpose — persuade, inform, entertain, request

Point out: Version 1 includes costs and a plan (because a parent cares about responsibility). Version 2 includes excitement and shared experiences (because a friend cares about fun). Version 3 includes lease terms and documentation (because a landlord cares about rules). You include what YOUR READER needs, not just what you want to say.

Step 3 — The Shapeshifter Worksheet (15 min)

Hand out the worksheet. Kids pick a message and write it for different audiences. See age notes below for how many audiences each tier writes for.

Step 4 — Find Your Voice (10 min)

Transition with: “We just learned how to change our writing for different readers. But underneath all that adapting, there is something that stays the same—YOUR voice. Your voice is how you naturally sound on paper. It is your personality, your rhythm, your word choices. It is what makes your writing sound like YOU.”

Each child free-writes for 5 minutes about something they care about. No rules, no audience—just them and the page. Then they read it back and underline 2–3 sentences that sound most like them.

Teaching Tip: Voice vs. Audience

Kids sometimes worry: “If I change my writing for different people, am I being fake?” Great question. The answer: No. You are being a communicator. You speak differently to your grandmother than to your best friend—but you are still YOU in both conversations. Your voice (humor, curiosity, passion) stays. The style (vocabulary, formality, detail) shifts. That is not dishonesty. It is skill.

Age-Tier Notes

Primary (K–1, age 6): Writes (or dictates) the same message 2 ways: to a friend and to a grown-up. Draws a picture for each version. Focus on the concept that we use different words for different people. Keep it simple and fun.
Elementary (3rd–4th, ages 9–10): Writes 2 audience versions (friend + parent or friend + teacher) plus the “Find Your Voice” free write exercise. Skip the detailed analysis section.
Middle School (5th–7th, ages 11–13): All 3 audience versions + the full analysis of what changed + the voice exercise. Should be able to articulate specific differences in vocabulary and tone.
High School (9th+, age 15): All 3 audience versions + full analysis + voice exercise + identify what aspects of voice stayed consistent across all 3 versions (this is sophisticated metacognition about their own writing identity).

What Success Looks Like

  • Kids can explain WHY they would write differently for different people
  • Their 2–3 audience versions are noticeably different in tone, vocabulary, and detail
  • They can identify at least 2 characteristics of their own writing voice
  • They understand that adapting to an audience is a skill, not fakery
