Lesson 3: The Hook — 8 Ways to Begin

Unit 2: Openings & Closings — Parent Guide

Duration: 35 minutes
Materials: This guide, Write Four Openings worksheet
Ages: All tiers (6–15)
Curriculum: Writing

Lesson Overview

The first sentence is the most important sentence in any piece of writing. It's the moment a reader decides to keep going or walk away. In this lesson, your kids will learn 8 different ways to hook a reader and practice writing multiple openings for the same topic — proving that there's never just one way to begin.

Lesson Flow

Step 1 — Read 4 Openings for the Same Topic (5 min)

Read all four openings below (persuasive topic first, then narrative topic). Don't reveal the hook type yet — just read them and ask: "Which one makes you want to read more? Why?"

Step 2 — Walk Through All 8 Hook Techniques (10 min)

Now go through each of the 8 hook types below. Read the name, the description, and the example. Have the kids notice which hooks were used in the Step 1 openings.

Step 3 — Write 4 Openings Worksheet (15 min)

Each child picks one topic and writes 4 different openings using 4 different hook techniques. The key insight: the same topic can begin in completely different ways, and each choice creates a different feeling.

Step 4 — Read Aloud & Family Vote (5 min)

Each person reads all 4 of their openings. Family votes on which one makes them want to read the rest of the piece. Record votes on the worksheet.

Same Topic, 4 Different Hooks — Persuasive

Topic: "Why financial literacy should be taught in schools"
Startling Fact Hook

"Seventy-eight percent of Americans live paycheck to paycheck, yet most schools never teach a single lesson about money."

Question Hook

"What if the most important skill for adult life was never taught in a single classroom?"

Bold Claim Hook

"Schools are failing an entire generation — not in math or reading, but in the one subject that affects every day of their adult lives."

Anecdote Hook

"When I was eighteen, I signed up for my first credit card because they gave me a free t-shirt. Three years later, I owed $4,000 in debt I didn't understand."

Same Topic, 4 Different Hooks — Narrative

Topic: "The time I got lost in the woods"
Action Hook

"I ran. Branches slapped my arms and roots grabbed at my feet, but I didn't stop — because the trail had disappeared twenty minutes ago, and the sun was going down."

Dialogue Hook

"'Stay on the path,' my dad said. 'You literally cannot get lost if you stay on the path.' I reminded myself of that three hours later, standing in a clearing I'd never seen before."

Sensory Image Hook

"The woods smelled like wet pine and rotting leaves, and somewhere behind me, a branch cracked — the kind of crack that means something heavy just stepped on it."

Mystery / Surprise Hook

"I wasn't supposed to be in the woods that day. No one was. And that's exactly why I went."

The 8 Hook Techniques

Walk through each of these during Step 2.

1 Startling Fact or Statistic
Hit the reader with a surprising number or fact they didn't know. Works best for persuasive and informational writing.
"Every year, Americans throw away 80 billion pounds of food — enough to fill 730 football stadiums."
2 Question
Ask a question the reader can't help wanting to answer. The question should feel genuinely interesting, not like a quiz.
"Have you ever wondered why we remember song lyrics from ten years ago but forget what we studied last week?"
3 Bold Claim or Controversial Statement
Make a strong assertion that demands attention. The reader thinks, "Wait, really?" and keeps reading to see if you can back it up.
"Homework does more harm than good, and the research proves it."
4 Anecdote (Short Story)
Open with a brief, real story that connects to your topic. Humans are wired for stories — we can't stop listening once one starts.
"My grandmother never went to college. She taught herself to read from a Bible and a Sears catalog. By the time I knew her, she'd read more books than anyone I've ever met."
5 Dialogue
Start with someone talking. Dialogue drops the reader into a scene instantly. Works especially well for narratives.
"'Don't open it,' she said. So naturally, I opened it."
6 Action
Start in the middle of something happening. No setup, no background — just throw the reader into motion.
"The ball left his hand, and for two full seconds, nobody in the gym breathed."
7 Sensory Image
Paint a vivid picture using one or more of the five senses. The reader should feel like they're there.
"The lake was so still that morning it looked like a sheet of black glass, and the only sound was the drip of water off the end of my paddle."
8 Mystery / Surprise
Open with something unexpected or unexplained. Create a gap in the reader's knowledge that they need to fill. Make them think, "Wait — what?"
"The letter arrived on a Tuesday, addressed to someone who had been dead for eleven years."

Age-Tier Notes

Primary (K–1, age 6): Focus on 3 simpler hook types: Question, Action, and Dialogue. They write 2 openings (not 4). Drawing space is provided on the worksheet — they can draw the scene their opening creates, then write (or dictate) the sentence. Keep it to 1–2 sentences per opening.
Elementary (3rd–4th, ages 9–10): Writes 3 openings using 3 different hook techniques. They can choose from any of the 8 hooks. Encourage them to try at least one they've never used before.
Middle School (5th–7th, ages 11–13): Writes all 4 openings. After the family vote, they take the winning hook and expand it into a full first paragraph (5–8 sentences).
High School (9th+, age 15): Writes all 4 openings plus the full paragraph expansion. Challenge: can they combine two hook techniques in a single opening? (e.g., an anecdote that starts with dialogue)

Facilitation Tips

Tip: Let Them Feel the Difference

Don't explain why one hook is better than another — let the kids feel it. When you read the four openings for the same topic, their reactions will tell you everything. "Which one grabbed you?" is more powerful than any lecture.

Tip: No Wrong Hooks

All 8 hook types work. The point of this lesson isn't to find the "best" hook — it's to learn that you have choices. Many kids start every piece with "In this essay I will..." or "One day, I..." This lesson breaks that habit.

Tip: The Voting Is the Learning

When family members explain why they voted for a particular opening, they're articulating what makes writing effective. That conversation is as valuable as the writing itself.

Tip: Connect to Reading

Grab a few books off the shelf and read just the first sentences. Which hook technique did each author use? This makes the lesson feel real — published authors use these exact techniques.

What to Look For

By the end of this lesson, each child should be able to:

  • Name at least 4 different ways to begin a piece of writing
  • Write multiple openings for the same topic using different hook techniques
  • Explain why one opening is more engaging than another
