The Feedback Framework

How to Help a Writer Get Better

The Problem: "I liked it" is the most useless feedback in the world. Here's how to actually help.
1

ONE THING THAT WORKED

Be specific. Not "it was good" but:

"The opening question hooked me because I immediately wanted to know the answer." • "The description of the burned toast made me smell it."

2

ONE THING I WANTED MORE OF

Not "it was bad" — frame it as curiosity:

"I wanted to SEE what the room looked like." • "I wanted to know how she felt when that happened." • "The middle section moved fast — I wanted more detail on the argument with her brother."

3

ONE QUESTION I HAD

Something that wasn't clear or that you're curious about:

"What happened next?" • "Why did he go back to the house?" • "How does this connect to your thesis?"

Sentence Starters — Praise

  • "I liked the part where... because..."
  • "The strongest sentence was... because..."
  • "I could really picture / feel / hear..."
  • "This reminded me of..."

Sentence Starters — Growth

  • "I wanted to know more about..."
  • "I got confused when..."
  • "What if you tried..."
  • "I was curious about..."

Rules of Feedback

  • Be kind AND honest (both, not either/or)
  • Talk about the WRITING, not the WRITER ("The ending felt rushed" not "You rushed the ending")
  • Be specific — vague praise is as useless as vague criticism
  • Ask questions more than give commands
  • Remember: you're helping them make THEIR piece better, not rewriting it as YOU would

Age Guidance

Ages 6–8: Just do Step 1 (one thing that worked) + one question. That's it!
Ages 9–12: All 3 steps, using the sentence starters above.
Ages 13+: Full framework. Aim for specificity — cite exact sentences and explain why they work (or don't).
