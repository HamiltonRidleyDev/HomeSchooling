Read Like a Writer — Recommended Reading List

Great writers are great readers. Reading fills the tank. Try to read at least 20 minutes a day — and when you find a sentence or paragraph that amazes you, mark it. That's you learning to write.

Ages 6–8 — Read-Aloud & Early Reader
Narrative / Story
Where the Wild Things Are — Maurice Sendak — A masterclass in emotion and imagination in under 400 words. Models: economy of language, emotional arc
Charlotte's Web — E.B. White — Perfect story structure with unforgettable characters. Models: narrative structure, character voice
The One and Only Ivan — Katherine Applegate — First-person voice from a gorilla, simple but powerful. Models: first-person voice, short chapters
Descriptive
Owl Moon — Jane Yolen — Gorgeous sensory writing about a winter night walk. Models: vivid description, sensory detail
Persuasive
I Wanna Iguana — Karen Kaufman Orloff — A kid writes persuasive letters to his parents. Hilarious and instructive. Models: persuasive argument, letter format
Poetry
Where the Sidewalk Ends — Shel Silverstein — Playful, witty poems that make kids want to write their own. Models: rhythm, wordplay, humor
Ages 9–11 — Independent Reader
Narrative / Story
Hatchet — Gary Paulsen — Survival story with spare, tense prose. Models: action writing, short sentences for tension
Wonder — R.J. Palacio — Multiple perspectives on the same events. Models: point of view, multi-narrator structure
Percy Jackson series — Rick Riordan — Fast-paced, funny first-person voice that hooks reluctant readers. Models: voice, pacing, humor in narration
Descriptive
Because of Winn-Dixie — Kate DiCamillo — Vivid Southern setting you can feel and smell. Models: setting description, sensory detail
Persuasive / Opinion
The Wild Robot — Peter Brown — Raises questions about nature vs. technology that spark debate. Models: thematic argument, showing vs. telling
Research / Nonfiction
Who Was... series — Various — Biography meets narrative nonfiction, great research models. Models: nonfiction structure, research into story
DK Eyewitness books — Various — Visual nonfiction that organizes complex information clearly. Models: informational organization, visual layout
Poetry
Love That Dog — Sharon Creech — A kid learning to love poetry, told entirely in verse. Models: voice, free verse, emotional honesty
Ages 12–15 — Advanced Reader
Narrative / Story
The Outsiders — S.E. Hinton (written at age 16!) — Proof that teenagers can write something that lasts forever. Models: authentic teen voice, narrative drive
Ender's Game — Orson Scott Card — Strategy, tension, and a protagonist who thinks. Models: pacing, internal conflict, plot twists
The Giver — Lois Lowry — Deceptively simple language carrying enormous ideas. Models: world-building, thematic depth, precise language
Descriptive / Prose Style
The Book Thief — Markus Zusak — Extraordinary prose narrated by Death. Every page teaches something about language. Models: metaphor, unique narrator, lyrical prose
Persuasive / Essay
I Am Malala — Malala Yousafzai — Personal story as persuasive argument for education. Models: personal narrative as argument, global perspective
Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass — Frederick Douglass — First-person testimony that changed a nation. Models: persuasive memoir, rhetorical power
Research / Nonfiction
Freakonomics for Young Readers — Levitt & Dubner — Data-driven arguments made fascinating. Models: evidence-based argument, surprising thesis
Unbroken (YA edition) — Laura Hillenbrand — Research brought to life as a riveting narrative. Models: narrative nonfiction, research as storytelling
Creative / Voice
The House on Mango Street — Sandra Cisneros — Short vignettes with incredible voice. Perfect mentor text. Models: vignette structure, distinctive voice, imagery
Personal Essay
This I Believe — anthology — Short personal essays on core beliefs. Great models for personal writing. Models: personal essay, concise argument, authentic voice
Poetry
Brown Girl Dreaming — Jacqueline Woodson — Memoir told in verse. Beautiful, accessible, and deeply personal. Models: verse memoir, rhythm, imagery
The Hill We Climb — Amanda Gorman — Proof that poetry can move the world. Models: rhetorical devices, performance writing, hope as argument

Tip: When you read something you love, ask: WHY do I love this? What is the writer doing? That's how you steal techniques (legally).
