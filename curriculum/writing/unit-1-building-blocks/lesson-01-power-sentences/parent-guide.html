Lesson 1: Power Sentences

Unit 1: Building Blocks — Parent Guide

Duration: 30 minutes
Materials: This guide, Sentence Surgery worksheet
Ages: All tiers (6–15)
Curriculum: Writing

Lesson Overview

Great writing starts at the sentence level. In this lesson, your kids will learn to transform flat, boring sentences into vivid, specific, muscular ones. The core idea: a power sentence replaces vague words with precise ones, trades weak verbs for strong ones, and adds sensory detail so the reader can see, hear, and feel what's happening.

Lesson Flow

Step 1 — Read Weak vs. Power Sentences Together (2 min)

Read the five mentor pairs below out loud. Read the weak version first, then the power version. Let the contrast speak for itself before you discuss anything.

Step 2 — Discuss What Changed (3 min)

After reading all five, ask: "What did you notice? What's different about the second versions?" Guide the conversation toward these four techniques:

  1. Specificity — replace vague words with exact ones
  2. Strong verbs — swap weak verbs for vivid, muscular ones
  3. Sensory detail — add what you see, hear, smell, touch, taste
  4. Show emotions through actions — don't label feelings, show them
Step 3 — Sentence Surgery Worksheet (15 min)

Hand out the Sentence Surgery worksheet. Each child rewrites weak sentences into power sentences. See age notes below for how many each tier completes.

Step 4 — Power Paragraph (10 min)

Each child writes 5 connected power sentences about something they love. The sentences should form a mini-paragraph, not just a random list. Encourage them to pick a topic they're passionate about — a pet, a sport, a food, a place.

Step 5 — Read Aloud & Family Vote

Everyone reads their power paragraph aloud. After each person reads, the family picks the single strongest sentence from that paragraph. Celebrate the wins. This is about noticing excellence, not correcting mistakes.

Mentor Text: Weak vs. Power Sentences

Read these aloud during Step 1. Pause between each pair so kids can hear the difference.

Weak

"The dog was big."

Power

"The Great Dane's head reached the kitchen counter, and when he wagged his tail, picture frames rattled on the shelf."

Weak

"She walked to school."

Power

"She shuffled along the cracked sidewalk, dragging her backpack straps through every puddle from last night's rain."

Weak

"The food was good."

Power

"The warm cornbread crumbled the moment she bit into it, and the honey butter melted across her tongue."

Weak

"He was sad."

Power

"He sat on the edge of his bed, turning his phone over and over in his hands, waiting for a message that never came."

Weak

"The room was messy."

Power

"Dirty socks draped over the lamp, three cereal bowls grew mold on the nightstand, and the carpet had vanished under a mountain of crumpled papers."

Age-Tier Notes

Primary (K–1, age 6): Does the first 5 sentences only. Word banks are provided on the worksheet so they can choose strong words rather than generating them from scratch. For the paragraph, they can dictate to you if writing is still slow. Focus on the idea of making sentences more interesting, not on spelling or handwriting.
Elementary (3rd–4th, ages 9–10): Does all 10 sentences. Encourage them to try at least two different techniques across their rewrites. For the paragraph, remind them that the sentences should connect — not just be five random facts.
Middle School (5th–7th, ages 11–13): Does all 10 sentences plus the paragraph. After each rewrite, they identify which technique they used (specificity, strong verb, sensory detail, or show-don't-tell). Push them to use multiple techniques in a single sentence.
High School (9th+, age 15): Does all 10 sentences plus the paragraph plus technique identification. Challenge them to vary sentence length and rhythm within their paragraph. Bonus: rewrite one weak sentence three different ways using three different techniques.

Facilitation Tips

Tip: Don't Over-Correct

This lesson is about generating vivid language, not about grammar or spelling. If a six-year-old writes "the dog jumpd over the big big fens," celebrate the vivid image. Fix spelling another day.

Tip: Model It Live

Before the kids start the worksheet, do one sentence together as a family. Take "The car was fast" and brainstorm power versions together on a whiteboard or scrap paper. Let the kids see your thinking process, including your bad first attempts.

Tip: Celebrate Specificity

When reading aloud, point out the most specific detail in each kid's writing. "I love that you said 'cherry Kool-Aid' instead of just 'juice.' That's exactly what a power sentence does — it makes the reader see the real thing."

Tip: The Primary Learner

Your youngest may need you to read the weak sentence and ask, "Can you think of a better word for 'big'?" Then let them pick from the word bank. Dictation is fine. The goal is to train their ear for strong language, even if their hand can't keep up yet.

What to Look For

By the end of this lesson, each child should be able to:

  • Explain in their own words why "The Great Dane knocked picture frames off the shelf" is stronger than "The dog was big"
  • Rewrite at least one weak sentence into a noticeably more vivid version
  • Use at least one of the four power sentence techniques on purpose
