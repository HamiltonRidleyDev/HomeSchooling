Lesson 2: Show Don't Tell

Unit 1: Building Blocks — Parent Guide

Duration: 35 minutes
Materials: This guide, Show Don't Tell worksheet
Ages: All tiers (6–15)
Curriculum: Writing

Lesson Overview

If Lesson 1 was about individual words, this lesson is about the strategy behind them. "Show don't tell" is the single most repeated piece of writing advice for a reason: showing makes the reader experience something; telling just informs them. Today your kids will learn to turn flat statements into vivid scenes.

Lesson Flow

Step 1 — Read the Mentor Examples Side by Side (5 min)

Read each telling version aloud, then immediately read the showing version. Let the kids react naturally. Don't explain yet — just let them feel the difference.

Step 2 — Discuss: What's Different? (5 min)

Ask: "What did the showing versions do that the telling versions didn't?" Guide toward these key insights:

  • Telling labels. It gives you the conclusion: "She was nervous."
  • Showing gives evidence. It describes what nervous looks like, sounds like, feels like.
  • Showing trusts the reader to figure out the emotion or quality from the evidence.
  • Showing uses the five senses — sight, sound, smell, touch, taste.
Step 3 — The Rewrite Challenge Worksheet (15 min)

Hand out the Show Don't Tell worksheet. Each child rewrites telling sentences into showing passages. See age notes below for tier assignments.

Step 4 — The Family Show-Down Game (10 min)

This is the fun part. Here's how it works:

  1. Parent reads a telling sentence aloud (use the ones from the worksheet or make up new ones).
  2. Everyone gets 60 seconds to write a showing version. Timer is strict!
  3. Everyone reads their version aloud.
  4. Family votes on the most vivid version (you can't vote for yourself).
  5. Track the winner on the scoring sheet (on the worksheet). Do 5 rounds.

Mentor Examples: Telling vs. Showing

Read these aloud during Step 1. Pause between each pair.

Telling

"She was nervous."

Showing

"Her fingers found the hem of her shirt and twisted it into a tight spiral. She checked the clock — only two minutes had passed."

Telling

"The pizza was delicious."

Showing

"Cheese stretched in long, golden strings as she pulled the first slice free. The crust crackled under her teeth, and the smell of garlic and basil filled the kitchen."

Telling

"It was a hot day."

Showing

"The asphalt shimmered like water in the distance, and the metal slide burned the back of her legs before she made it halfway down."

Telling

"The old man was lonely."

Showing

"He set two places at the table every evening, even though the second chair had been empty for three years. Sometimes he'd talk to it anyway — just to hear a voice in the room."

Telling

"The garden was beautiful."

Showing

"Sunflowers leaned toward the fence like curious neighbors, and a tangle of purple morning glories climbed the trellis. Bees hummed between the tomato plants, and the air smelled like warm dirt and lavender."

Telling

"He was really excited about the gift."

Showing

"He tore the wrapping paper in two ragged strips, then froze when he saw the box. His mouth fell open. He looked up at his mom, back at the box, then let out a sound that was half laugh, half scream."

Age-Tier Notes

Primary (K–1, age 6): Does 4 simpler sentences. The worksheet includes picture prompts — they draw the scene first, then describe what they drew. Drawing first helps them generate sensory details. They may dictate their "showing" version to you.
Elementary (3rd–4th, ages 9–10): Does 6 rewrites plus the Five Senses paragraph. Remind them that "showing" doesn't have to be long — even two well-chosen details can be powerful.
Middle School (5th–7th, ages 11–13): Does all 8 rewrites plus the Five Senses paragraph. After each rewrite, they identify which senses they used. Push them to include at least 2 senses per rewrite.
High School (9th+, age 15): Does all 8 rewrites plus the Five Senses paragraph plus sense identification. Challenge: at least one rewrite should use only action and dialogue (no adjectives or adverbs) to show the emotion.

Facilitation Tips

Tip: The Camera Test

Tell your kids: "If a camera could record it, it's showing. If a camera can't record it, it's telling." A camera can record someone twisting their shirt hem. A camera can't record "she was nervous." This is the simplest way to explain show vs. tell.

Tip: Telling Isn't Always Bad

For your older kids, mention that telling has its place. Sometimes you need to move a story forward quickly: "Three days passed." You don't show every minute of those three days. The skill is knowing when to show and when to tell. Show the moments that matter most.

Tip: The Show-Down Game

This game works best when you play too. Write your own versions and read them aloud. When kids see you struggle with the 60-second timer, they realize writing is hard for everyone. Keep the energy fun and competitive, not judgmental.

Tip: Encourage Weird Details

The best showing often comes from unexpected, specific details. "The burned smell of the toaster" is more vivid than "the kitchen smelled." Celebrate strange, precise observations.

What to Look For

By the end of this lesson, each child should be able to:

  • Explain the difference between telling and showing in their own words
  • Rewrite a "telling" sentence into a "showing" passage with at least two sensory details
  • Identify whether a sentence is telling or showing
