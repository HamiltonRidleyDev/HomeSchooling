Lesson 10: The Review + Family Debate

Unit 5: Persuasion  |  Time: 45 minutes total (25 min writing + 20 min debate)  |  Ages: 6–15 (all tiers)  |  Materials: Review Worksheet, Debate Prep Sheet, a sample review to read aloud
The Big Idea: This lesson pairs written persuasion (the review) with oral persuasion (the debate). Writing a review teaches kids to support opinions with evidence. The debate forces them to think on their feet. Together, they build the full persuasion toolkit.
Part A: The Review (25 Minutes)

Each kid writes a review of something they actually use — a game, book, movie, restaurant, product, or app. The review should be honest, specific, and balanced. Then it gets posted or shared somewhere real.

1 Read a Great Review Together (5 min)
Pull up a well-written review (Amazon, Google, a book review site). Read it aloud and notice:
  • Does it state a clear opinion early?
  • Does it use specific evidence (not just “it’s great”)?
  • Is it balanced — does it mention both pros AND cons?
  • Does it end with a recommendation?
2 Pick Something to Review (5 min)
Each kid chooses something they know well enough to evaluate honestly. They should have used/read/watched/played it enough to give specific details.
3 Write the Review (15 min)
Using the Review Writer worksheet. Remind them: a review with ONLY praise is an ad, not a review. Honest criticism builds credibility.
Tip — Publishing: Post it somewhere real! Options: Amazon review, Google review, Goodreads, a family blog, read aloud at dinner, or written on a card to hand to a restaurant manager. Real audiences, real effort.
Part B: Family Debate (20 Minutes)

Pick a fun, arguable topic. Split into two sides. Argue your case out loud. This is loud, funny, and surprisingly educational.

1 Pick a Debate Topic (1 min)
Choose from the list below or invent your own. The best topics have no right answer.
2 Split into Two Sides (1 min)
Divide the family. For extra learning, assign kids to argue for a side they DON’T personally agree with — this builds empathy and critical thinking.
3 Prepare Arguments (3 min)
Each side huddles and uses the Debate Prep Sheet. Identify your 3 strongest arguments and anticipate what the other side will say.
4 Debate! (6 min)
Each side gets 2 minutes to present their arguments, then 1 minute for rebuttal. Use a timer. Encourage eye contact, clear speaking, and addressing the other side’s points.
5 Vote (2 min)
Family votes on which side ARGUED better — not which side they personally agree with. Discuss: what made that side more convincing?

Debate Topics

Dogs vs. Cats Summer vs. Winter Books vs. Movies Homework: Ban It or Keep It? Kids Should Choose Their Own Bedtime Breakfast vs. Dinner: Most Important Meal Better to Be Oldest vs. Youngest Sibling Save All Your Money vs. Enjoy Some Now

Tip: If the debate gets heated (it will), that’s great — passion is persuasive. But enforce one rule: you must address the OTHER side’s argument, not just repeat your own louder. Saying “You said X, but actually…” is more persuasive than just shouting your point again.

Age-Tier Adaptations

PRIMARY (Age 6, K–1)
Review: Draws a picture of the thing they’re reviewing and dictates 2–3 sentences (“I like it because…” and “One thing I don’t like is…”). Stars rating is fun for this age.
Debate: Participates by saying one reason for their side. No prep sheet needed — just stand up and say why.
ELEMENTARY (Ages 9–10, 3rd–4th)
Review: Writes a 1-paragraph review with at least 2 specific pros, 1 con, and a recommendation. Posts or shares it somewhere.
Debate: Full participation in the debate. Uses the prep sheet to write down 2–3 arguments.
MIDDLE SCHOOL (Ages 11–13, 5th–7th)
Review: Writes the full multi-paragraph review with hook, specific evidence, balanced analysis, and recommendation.
Debate: Full debate with prep sheet. Expected to directly address the other side’s arguments during rebuttal.
HIGH SCHOOL (Age 15, 9th+)
Review: Writes a polished, publishable review. Should read like something you’d find on a credible review site. Posts it publicly.
Debate: Full debate with prep sheet. Can serve as moderator/judge for younger kids’ rounds, or debate a parent for extra challenge.

What to Look For

In the Review:

  • Is the opinion clearly stated (not hidden)?
  • Are the pros backed by specific details?
  • Is there at least one honest criticism?
  • Would this help someone make a decision?

In the Debate:

  • Did they state a clear position?
  • Did they use reasons (not just feelings)?
  • Did they respond to the other side’s arguments?
  • Were they respectful even when disagreeing?
