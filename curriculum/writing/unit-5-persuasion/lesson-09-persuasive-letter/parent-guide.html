Lesson 9: The Persuasive Letter ACTUALLY SENT

Unit 5: Persuasion  |  Time: 40 minutes  |  Ages: 6–15 (all tiers)  |  Materials: Worksheet, envelopes/stamps or email access, sample persuasive letter
The Big Idea: This letter gets ACTUALLY MAILED or EMAILED. A real audience transforms effort. When kids know a real human being will read their words, everything changes: handwriting improves, arguments sharpen, tone becomes more thoughtful. Do not skip the sending step.

Lesson Flow (40 Minutes)

1 Read & Annotate a Great Persuasive Letter (5 min)
Read a persuasive letter aloud together. As you read, have kids call out the techniques they notice. Annotate or mark these on a shared copy:
  • Hook — What grabs attention in the first line?
  • Claim — What does the writer want?
  • Evidence — What facts, examples, or reasons support the claim?
  • Counterargument — Does the writer address what someone might say against them?
  • Call to Action — What specific thing does the writer ask the reader to DO?
2 Pick a REAL Topic (5 min)
Each kid picks something they genuinely want to change or request. It must be real — not hypothetical. See the topic list below for ideas. Help them identify who would actually receive this letter.
3 Plan Using the Worksheet (10 min)
Kids fill out the Persuasive Letter Planner: their claim, three pieces of evidence, counterargument and rebuttal, and their call to action. Walk around and help younger kids articulate their reasons.
4 Write the Letter (15 min)
Using the formal letter template on the worksheet, kids draft their letter. Remind them: respectful tone is non-negotiable. You can be firm and passionate without being rude.
5 Peer Review & Revise (5 min)
Swap letters with a sibling. The reviewer answers: Is the claim clear? Is the evidence convincing? Would YOU be persuaded? What’s the strongest part? What could be stronger? Then revise based on feedback.

The Structure of a Persuasive Letter

  1. Greeting — Formal: “Dear [Name/Title],”
  2. Opening Paragraph — Hook + Clear Claim (what you want and why you’re writing)
  3. Body Paragraph(s) — Evidence #1, #2, #3 — each with a specific fact, example, or reason
  4. Counterargument Paragraph — “Some might say…” + your rebuttal
  5. Closing Paragraph — Call to Action (what specifically you want them to do) + thank them
  6. Formal Closing — “Sincerely,” / “Respectfully,” + your name

Formal Letter Format

Your Name
Your Address
City, State, ZIP
Date

Recipient’s Name
Their Title (if applicable)
Organization
Address

Dear [Mr./Ms./Dr. Last Name],

[Body of letter]

Sincerely,
[Your Name]

Tip — Maintaining Respectful Tone: Teach kids the difference between assertive and aggressive. Model phrases like: “I respectfully request…” “I understand that… however…” “I appreciate your time and consideration.” Being polite makes you MORE persuasive, not less. Nobody ever changed their mind because someone yelled at them.

Age-Tier Adaptations

PRIMARY (Age 6, K–1)
Draws a picture of what they want. Dictates three reasons to a parent, who writes them as a letter together. The child signs their name and helps put it in the envelope or press “send.” Focus: understanding that writing can make things happen in the real world.
ELEMENTARY (Ages 9–10, 3rd–4th)
Writes a 3-paragraph letter: (1) claim + why I’m writing, (2) two strong reasons with details, (3) closing with call to action. Uses the formal greeting and closing. Does not need to address a counterargument yet.
MIDDLE SCHOOL (Ages 11–13, 5th–7th)
Writes the full persuasive letter with all elements: hook, claim, three evidence points, counterargument with rebuttal, and specific call to action. Uses formal letter format throughout.
HIGH SCHOOL (Age 15, 9th+)
Writes the full persuasive letter AND researches their evidence from real sources. Cites at least one fact, statistic, or expert opinion. The letter should be polished enough to send without embarrassment to any professional recipient.
After the Lesson: Actually send the letters. Today. Not “later.” Address envelopes, buy stamps, or open email. If a kid writes to a company, help them find the customer service address. If they write to local government, find the council member’s contact. The sending is the lesson.

What to Look For

  • Is the claim stated clearly in the first paragraph?
  • Are the evidence points specific (not vague)?
  • Does the tone stay respectful throughout?
  • Is there a clear, specific call to action?
  • Does the letter follow formal letter format?
  • Would YOU be persuaded if you received this?
