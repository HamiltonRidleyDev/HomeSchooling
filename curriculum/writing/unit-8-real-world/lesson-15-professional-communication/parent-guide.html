Lesson 15: Professional Communication

Unit 8: Real-World Writing — Parent Guide

Duration: 35 minutes
Materials: This guide, Professional Writing worksheet
Ages: All tiers (6–15)
Curriculum: Writing

Lesson Overview

This is writing that gets things DONE. Emails, messages, thank-you notes, cover letters — real-world writing that people actually read and respond to (or don't, if it's bad). The goal: every student writes at least one message today that gets actually sent to a real person.

This lesson is immediately practical. For the 15-year-old, the cover letter section is genuine college/job prep. For everyone, learning to communicate clearly and professionally is a life skill that starts now.

Lesson Flow

Step 1 — The Professional Email Test (5 min)

Show both emails below side by side. Read the bad one first, then the good one. Ask: "If you got both of these, which one would you respond to? Why?"

Let them articulate the differences before you name them. They'll usually spot: the good one has a subject line, a specific question, context, and is polite without being fake.

Bad Email
Subject: (none)
hey can u help me with something plz thx
Good Email
Subject: Question about Tuesday's practice schedule
Hi Coach Martinez, I wanted to confirm whether practice is at 3:30 or 4:00 on Tuesday. I have a dentist appointment at 2:00 and want to make sure I'll be back in time. Thank you, Jordan

Key differences to highlight:

  • The good email has a clear subject line — the reader knows what it's about before opening
  • The purpose is in the first sentence — no guessing
  • It provides context — why they're asking
  • There's a specific ask — easy to respond to
  • It's polite but not groveling — professional, not performative
Step 2 — The Anatomy of a Good Email (5 min)

Walk through the six parts of an effective email:

  1. Subject line — specific, short, tells the reader what to expect
  2. Greeting — "Hi [Name]," or "Dear [Name]," — match the formality to the relationship
  3. Purpose in the first sentence — don't bury the lead
  4. Brief body — provide necessary context, but be concise
  5. Clear ask — what do you need from this person? Make it easy to say yes
  6. Professional closing — "Thank you," / "Best," / "Sincerely," + your name
Step 3 — Write 3 Real Messages (20 min)

Using the worksheet, each student writes real messages that can be actually sent. The three messages are:

  1. The Polite Request — email to a teacher, coach, librarian, or business
  2. The Thank-You — genuine thank-you note to someone who helped them recently
  3. The Professional Introduction (ages 13+) — email introducing themselves for a job, volunteer opportunity, or internship. OR for ages 9–10: a message to a family member they haven't talked to recently.

The High School bonus: The 15-year-old also writes a cover letter. Walk them through the structure: who you are + what you're applying for, why you're a good fit (2–3 specific reasons), call to action + thank you.

Step 4 — Review Each Other's Messages (5 min)

Siblings swap and read each other's messages. The test: "Would you respond to this? Would you take this person seriously?" Quick fixes, then the messages get SENT.

Teaching Tips
  • Make it real. The power of this lesson is that the messages actually get sent. Help them identify real recipients. A thank-you to Grandma. An email to their piano teacher. A question to the library.
  • Formal vs. Casual — it depends. The same kid who texts "lol k" to friends needs to write "Thank you for your time" to a potential employer. The skill is knowing WHEN to switch.
  • Don't over-formalize. "Dear Sir/Madam, I am writing to inquire..." is outdated and stiff. Modern professional writing is clear, warm, and direct.
  • Text message etiquette matters too. Even casual messages have a spectrum. "k" to your friend is fine. "k" to your coach after they explain practice changes is not.

Additional Examples

Requesting Information from a Business

Bad
Subject: question
do u guys do birthday parties? how much??
Good
Subject: Birthday party availability — Saturday, April 12
Hi there, I'm interested in hosting a birthday party for my 10-year-old at your facility on Saturday, April 12. Could you let me know about available packages and pricing for a group of about 12 kids? Thank you, Sarah Mitchell

Thank-You Note

Example
Subject: Thank you for the recommendation letter
Dear Mrs. Chen, Thank you so much for writing a recommendation letter for my summer program application. I know it took time out of your schedule, and it means a lot that you were willing to do that for me. I'll let you know how it goes! Gratefully, Marcus

Cover Letter Structure (High School)

Example Structure
Paragraph 1 — Opening: "My name is [Name], and I'm writing to apply for [position] at [organization]. I learned about this opportunity through [source]." Paragraph 2 — Why I'm a Good Fit: "I would be a strong candidate because [specific reason #1], [specific reason #2], and [specific reason #3]." Use concrete examples, not just adjectives. Paragraph 3 — Closing: "I would love the opportunity to discuss this further. I'm available [days/times] and can be reached at [contact info]. Thank you for your time and consideration."

Age-Tier Adjustments

Primary (Age 6): Writes (or dictates) a thank-you card to someone specific. Draws a picture on it. Includes at least one sentence about WHY they're thankful. It gets delivered.
Elementary (Ages 9–10): Writes 2 messages: a polite request + a thank-you. Skip the professional introduction; instead, write a friendly message to a family member. Does the formality spectrum activity.
Middle School (Ages 11–13): Writes all 3 messages. Does the text message formality spectrum. Messages should be polished enough to actually send.
High School (Age 15): Writes all 3 messages + the cover letter + the text message spectrum analysis. The cover letter should be for a real (or realistic) opportunity. Push for specific, concrete language — not "I'm a hard worker" but "I volunteered 40 hours at the food bank last summer and managed the intake desk independently."

Materials Needed

  • Professional Writing worksheet (1 per student)
  • Pencils/pens
  • For Primary: blank card or folded paper + colored pencils/crayons
  • Access to email/phone for actually sending messages (after the lesson)
