Lesson 16: The Personal Essay + Presentation

Unit 8: Real-World Writing — Parent Guide — CAPSTONE

Duration: 4 sessions (~100 min total)
Materials: This guide, Brainstorm worksheet, Essay Draft worksheet
Ages: All tiers (6–15)
Curriculum: Writing — Final Lesson

This Is the Capstone

Everything they've learned comes together in one piece of writing. Power sentences, show don't tell, hooks, bookend conclusions, revision, voice — all of it. This is the culmination of the entire curriculum.

Lesson Overview

The personal essay is the most important form of writing your kids will ever do. College applications, scholarship essays, job interviews that ask "tell me about yourself," personal statements for programs and opportunities — this format shows up everywhere. But more than that, it teaches them to find meaning in their own experiences and communicate it with clarity and honesty.

For the 15-year-old, this IS college essay prep. For everyone, it's a milestone in self-expression: the moment they write something truly personal, revise it until it shines, and then stand up and deliver it to the family.

What Makes a Great Personal Essay

  • Specificity over generality. Not "I learned a lot from sports" but "the moment Coach Williams made me run the play again for the fifth time, and I finally understood what she meant by 'trust the process.'"
  • Vulnerability. The best essays show real feelings, real struggles, real uncertainty. Not weakness — honesty.
  • A moment of change or realization. Something shifted. The writer saw the world differently after this experience.
  • YOUR voice, not a "writing voice." It should sound like THEM, not like someone trying to sound smart.
Common Pitfalls to Watch For
  • Too broad. "My family is important to me" covers everything and says nothing. Find the ONE moment that shows it.
  • Trying to impress instead of express. Thesaurus abuse, grand statements, philosophical conclusions that don't feel earned. Strip it back.
  • Summarizing instead of showing. "I was really nervous" vs. "My hands were shaking so hard I could hear my paper crinkling." (Lesson 2 all over again.)
  • The trophy essay. "I won the championship and learned teamwork" is the most common personal essay in the world. Push past it. What happened AFTER the trophy? What did the loss feel like? What did they learn in practice, not at the game?
Session 1 — Brainstorm (20 min)
Explore Topics Using the Brainstorm Worksheet

The brainstorm worksheet gives 10 prompts. Students explore 3–5 of them, writing 2–3 sentences for each. The goal isn't to pick the "best" topic — it's to find the one that feels the most real. The one they have the most to say about. The one that makes them slightly uncomfortable to share — that's usually the one.

Read Mentor Personal Essays

Read 2–3 short personal essay excerpts together. Point out:

  • How they start with a specific scene, not a thesis statement
  • How they weave reflection into narrative
  • How the endings connect back to the beginning
What to Look for in Mentor Essays

Find 2–3 short personal essays appropriate for your family. Good sources include: published college application essays (many are available online), personal essays from magazines, or essays from books like "This I Believe." Read them aloud and discuss: What makes you FEEL something? What specific details stick with you? Where does the writer show vulnerability?

Session 2 — Draft (30 min)
Write the Full Essay

Using the Essay Draft worksheet, students write their complete essay. Remind them to use every technique from the curriculum:

  • Hook opening (Lesson 3) — drop us into a moment
  • Show don't tell (Lesson 2) — sensory details, actions over labels
  • Power sentences (Lesson 1) — strong verbs, specific nouns
  • Funnel or narrative structure (Lesson 4)
  • Dialogue where appropriate (Lesson 14)
  • Bookend conclusion (Lesson 4) — echo the opening
  • Their authentic voice (Lesson 6)

Don't stop to edit. Just write. The revision comes later.

Session 3 — Feedback (20 min)
Peer Review with TWO Siblings

Each student gets feedback from two different siblings. Two perspectives catch more than one. Use the peer review section on the draft worksheet. After receiving feedback, students revise: the changes they make based on feedback should be documented.

Feedback Ground Rules
  • Be honest but kind. "This part confused me" is better than "this is bad."
  • Be specific. "I loved the part about the kitchen" is more useful than "it was good."
  • Ask questions. "What did you mean by this?" helps the writer see what's unclear.
  • Personal essays are PERSONAL. Treat the content with respect.
Session 4 — Final Draft + Presentation (30 min)
Polish the Final Draft (15 min)

Apply the full revision checklist from the worksheet. Use everything from Lesson 5 (the 20% cut). Read it aloud to yourself — does it flow? Does it sound like you?

Deliver It (15 min)

Each student reads their essay aloud to the family. This is the presentation component:

  • Eye contact — look up from the page at least 3 times
  • Pacing — slow down at the important parts
  • Vocal variety — don't read in monotone
  • Own the words — this is YOUR story, be present in it

After each presentation, family responds with one specific thing that resonated. No generic "that was good" — name the line, the moment, the feeling.

Make It Special

This is the final lesson. Treat it like a milestone. After all presentations, consider adding these essays to a Family Writing Anthology — a collection of everyone's best work from the entire curriculum. Print it, bind it, keep it. These words matter.

Age-Tier Adjustments

Primary (Age 6): Tells their story aloud while a parent writes it down. They illustrate it with drawings. At the presentation, they "read" their story (even if they need help with words) and show their illustrations. The story can be as simple as "my favorite day" or "something that made me happy."
Elementary (Ages 9–10): Writes 1 page (200–400 words). Uses the brainstorm prompts and planner. Reads aloud to the family. Focus on having a clear beginning, middle, and ending with one specific moment.
Middle School (Ages 11–13): Writes 2 pages (400–700 words). Full brainstorm, draft, peer review, and revision process. Presents with some eye contact and vocal variety. Push for vulnerability and specificity.
High School (Age 15): Writes the full essay (600–1000 words). This should be college-essay quality. Full process including revision checklist and two rounds of peer feedback. Delivers it as a polished presentation with confident delivery. This is the real deal.

Materials Needed

  • Brainstorm worksheet (1 per student)
  • Essay Draft worksheet (1 per student)
  • Pencils/pens + erasers
  • For Primary: blank paper + colored pencils for illustrations
  • 2–3 mentor personal essays to read aloud (find age-appropriate examples)
  • Timer for presentation practice
  • Optional: a nice folder or binder for the Family Writing Anthology
