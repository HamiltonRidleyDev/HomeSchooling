Before You Begin

Your job is to be a listener, not a talker. Ask a question, then let them talk. Write down as much as you can—especially their exact words (you will need these for dialogue in your story). If they give a short answer, use the follow-up prompts to dig deeper.

Starter Questions

Pick 5–7 questions from this list. Check the ones you plan to ask. You do not need to ask them all—one great question can lead to the whole story.

“Tell me about a time in your life when everything changed.”
“What's the scariest thing that ever happened to you?”
“What's the happiest moment you remember?”
“Tell me about a time you failed at something and what you learned.”
“What was life like when you were my age?”
“Tell me about a person who changed your life.”
“What's the hardest decision you ever had to make?”
“What's something most people don't know about you?”
“If you could relive one day, which would it be?”
“What advice would you give your younger self?”

During the Interview — Capture Everything

Write fast. You can clean it up later. Focus on getting the details that will make your story come alive.

Key Details

What did they see, hear, feel? What specific things do they remember?

 

Exact Words (Quotes for Dialogue)

Write down the exact things they say—these become dialogue in your story. Use quotation marks.

 

The Setting

Where were they? What did it look like? Time of day? Weather? Surroundings?

 

The Emotion

How did they feel? How did they SHOW that feeling? (Did their voice change? Did they cry? Laugh? Go quiet?)

 

The Turning Point

What changed? What was the moment when the story shifted?

 

Follow-Up Prompts

When they give a short answer, use these to dig deeper. The best details come from follow-up questions.

To get more detail:
  • “Can you tell me more about that?”
  • “What happened right BEFORE that?”
  • “What did you see / hear / smell?”
To get emotion:
  • “What did that feel like?”
  • “Were you scared / excited / surprised?”
  • “How did you react in the moment?”
To get dialogue:
  • “What did you say? What did they say back?”
  • “Do you remember the exact words?”
  • “What did people around you say?”
To find the turning point:
  • “What changed after that?”
  • “When did you realize things were different?”
  • “What would have happened if you had made a different choice?”
