Lesson 8: The Family Story Project

Unit 4: Storytelling — Parent Guide

Duration: Multi-session (Interview 20 min + Draft 30 min + Revision 20 min)
Materials: This guide, Interview Guide worksheet, Story Draft worksheet
Ages: All tiers (6–15)
Curriculum: Writing

Lesson Overview

This is the capstone project for the storytelling unit. Each child interviews one family member about a memorable life moment, then writes it as a narrative story—not “Grandma said she was scared” but actually telling the story with scene, dialogue, and detail.

The deliverable is a written story they give to the person they interviewed. This becomes a family keepsake. It is real writing for a real audience with a real purpose. That combination is rare in school assignments, and it matters.

Why This Project Is Special

This project ties together every skill from the curriculum: strong verbs (L1), sensory detail (L2), hooks (L3), showing not telling (L4), revision (L5), audience awareness (L6), and economy of language (L7). It also adds a new skill: dialogue. And it produces something the family will keep forever.

Session 1: The Interview (20 minutes)
Setup (2 min)

Each child picks a family member to interview: parent, grandparent, sibling, aunt, uncle, or family friend. The interview can happen in person or by phone/video call. Hand out the Interview Guide worksheet.

The Interview (15 min)

Kids use the starter questions on the worksheet to guide the conversation. The key skill here is listening and asking follow-up questions—not just writing down answers.

Debrief (3 min)

After the interview, each child shares one surprising thing they learned. This builds excitement for the writing session.

Interview Tips for Kids
  • Listen more than you talk. Your job is to draw the story out, not fill the silence.
  • Ask follow-up questions. When someone says “It was scary,” ask: “What did scary feel like? What did you see? What did you do?”
  • Capture exact words. If they say something memorable, write down their exact words in quotes. These become your dialogue.
  • Get the five senses. Ask: “What did you see? Hear? Smell?” These details make the story come alive.
  • Find the turning point. Every good story has a moment when things changed. Ask: “What happened that changed everything?”
Session 2: The Draft (30 minutes)
Dialogue Mini-Lesson (8 min)

Before they start writing, teach the basics of dialogue. This is new content—spend time on it.

Dialogue Rules

  1. Quotation marks go around the spoken words only.
  2. Comma before the dialogue tag (inside the quotation marks).
  3. New speaker = new paragraph. Every time a different person talks, start a new line.
  4. Dialogue tags identify who is speaking: “she said,” “he whispered.”

Example

“I never thought I'd see you here,” she said, setting down her coffee.

“Neither did I,” he replied. “But here we are.”

“Said” Alternatives

Instead of “said” Try (when it fits)
Quiet whispered, murmured, muttered, breathed
Loud shouted, yelled, called, exclaimed
Emotional cried, laughed, sighed, groaned
Questioning asked, wondered, demanded, pressed
Important

Don't overdo the alternatives. “Said” is invisible—readers' eyes skip right over it, which is exactly what you want most of the time. Use alternatives sparingly for emphasis, not on every line.

Making Characters Sound Different

Real people don't all talk the same way. A grandmother might use longer sentences and old-fashioned words. A little kid might use short sentences and get excited easily. Think about how your interviewee actually talks and try to capture that rhythm on the page.

Writing Time (20 min)

Using their interview notes and the Story Draft worksheet, kids write the story as a narrative. Remind them: this is not a report (“My grandma told me about the time she...”). This is a story—written as if the reader is there, watching it happen.

Requirements:

  • Start with a hook (reference L3 techniques)
  • Include at least 2 lines of dialogue
  • Use showing, not telling (reference L4)
  • Have a clear beginning, middle, and end
Quick Check (2 min)

Before ending the session, each child reads their first paragraph aloud. Give quick feedback: Does it hook the reader? Does the dialogue sound natural?

Session 3: Revision & Sharing (20 minutes)
Revision Using L5 Techniques (12 min)

Kids apply the revision checklist from Lesson 5 to their family story:

  1. Read aloud — mark awkward spots
  2. Strong verb hunt — replace at least 2 weak verbs
  3. Check dialogue punctuation
  4. The “so what?” test — does every paragraph earn its place?
  5. First sentence scan — do the openers flow?
Read Aloud & Gift (8 min)

If possible, each child reads their finished story aloud to the person they interviewed. If the person is not available, read it to the family. Then give the finished story to the interviewee as a gift.

This is the most important part. The story has a real reader. It matters. Celebrate it.

Making It a Keepsake
  • Consider having kids write or print a final “clean copy” on nice paper
  • Add a title page with the interviewee's name and the date
  • Older kids could type and format it like a real article or short story
  • If the interviewee is far away, mail it or read it over a video call

Age-Tier Notes

Primary (K–1, age 6): Draws the story in 4 panels (comic-strip style) with captions under each panel. Include at least one line of dialogue in a speech bubble. The parent can help with writing/spelling. The interview can be done together with the parent helping ask questions.
Elementary (3rd–4th, ages 9–10): Writes 1 page using the guided prompts on the Story Draft worksheet. Include 2 lines of dialogue. Use the planning section to organize before writing. Revision focuses on dialogue punctuation and one strong verb replacement.
Middle School (5th–7th, ages 11–13): Writes 2+ pages with all required elements: hook, dialogue (3+ lines), showing not telling, sensory details, clear structure. Full revision using the L5 checklist. Should aim for the story to read like a real narrative, not a report.
High School (9th+, age 15): Writes 2–3 pages with sophisticated narrative techniques: varied dialogue tags, scene-setting, internal thoughts, a thematic thread. The revision should include attention to pacing and voice. The finished product should read like a published personal narrative or oral history piece.

What Success Looks Like

  • Every child has a finished story based on a real interview
  • The stories include at least 2 lines of correctly punctuated dialogue
  • The interviewee recognizes their story and feels honored by it
  • Kids have experienced the full writing process: research (interview) → plan → draft → revise → share
  • The family has new keepsake stories that preserve real memories
