Lesson 7: 6-Word Story Challenge

Unit 4: Storytelling — Parent Guide

Duration: 30 minutes
Materials: This guide, Six-Word Stories worksheet
Ages: All tiers (6–15)
Curriculum: Writing

Lesson Overview

Ernest Hemingway allegedly wrote a complete story in just six words: “For sale: baby shoes, never worn.” Whether or not the attribution is real, the concept is powerful: a complete narrative—with characters, conflict, and emotion—in six words.

This lesson teaches economy—the art of making every word carry weight. It is the opposite of everything else in school, where kids are usually told “write MORE.” Here the constraint forces creativity. When you only have six words, you cannot waste a single one.

Why This Works

Six-word stories are the perfect low-stakes, high-reward writing exercise. They take seconds to write, so kids will produce many. The constraint makes it feel like a game, not an assignment. And the best ones can be genuinely powerful—kids feel like real writers when they nail one.

15 Example Six-Word Stories

Read these aloud before the writing begins. After each one, pause and ask: “What is the story behind these six words?”

  1. “For sale: baby shoes, never worn.” — attributed to Hemingway
  2. “Strangers. Friends. Best friends. Lovers. Strangers.”
  3. “Sorry, soldiers. We're out of medals.”
  4. “Born a twin. Died an only.”
  5. “Found true love. Married someone else.”
  6. “Mom called. I didn't pick up.”
  7. “'Wrong number,' says a familiar voice.”
  8. “Invented a time machine. Regret everything.”
  9. “Teacher said I couldn't. I did.”
  10. “Started a diet. Bought bigger fridge.”
  11. “Imaginary friend won't leave. I'm forty.”
  12. “Dog learned to open the door.”
  13. “Asked for a puppy. Got socks.”
  14. “Finally spoke up. Room went silent.”
  15. “Planted a seed. Grew a forest.”

Lesson Flow

Step 1 — Share & Discuss Examples (5 min)

Read Hemingway's story first. Ask: “What is the full story here? What happened?” Let kids fill in the implied narrative (a baby who was lost, or never came home, or outgrew the shoes before wearing them). The point: the six words are the tip of the iceberg. The real story lives underneath.

Then read 5–8 more examples. Let kids react. Some are funny, some are heartbreaking. Both count. Ask which ones hit hardest and why.

Step 2 — Write 10 Six-Word Stories (10 min)

Set a timer. Tell kids: “Write as many as you can. Don't overthink it. Some will be terrible. That's fine. Just keep going.” The goal is quantity first, quality second. The best ones often come after the first 4–5 “obvious” ones are out of the way.

Step 3 — Pick Your Best 3 & Family Vote (5 min)

Each child picks their 3 favorites and reads them aloud to the family. Everyone votes on the most powerful one. This is fun, collaborative, and builds confidence. Celebrate the winners. (Every kid should get genuine positive feedback on at least one story.)

Step 4 — The Expansion (10 min)

Each child takes their winning 6-word story and expands it: first into a paragraph (5–8 sentences), then into a full page. This teaches an essential lesson: you can always start small and grow. Starting with 6 words is sometimes easier than staring at a blank page.

This also reinforces every skill from the curriculum so far: strong verbs, showing not telling, sensory details, hooks.

Coaching Tips
  • If a kid is stuck: Suggest they think of a moment when everything changed. Or start with an emotion and work backward: What is a six-word story about surprise? About disappointment? About hope?
  • Count carefully: Contractions count as one word. Hyphenated words count as one. Articles (a, the) count—which is why many six-word stories skip them.
  • Encourage range: Some kids will only write funny ones, some only serious. Challenge them to try both.

Age-Tier Notes

Primary (K–1, age 6): Writes 3-word stories instead of 6-word. Draw the story first, then describe it in 3 words. Examples: “Cat ate homework.” “Balloon flew away.” “Best birthday ever.” The concept of economy still applies—just with a gentler constraint.
Elementary (3rd–4th, ages 9–10): Writes 6-word stories (aim for 6–8 of them). Picks a favorite and expands it into a paragraph (5–8 sentences). The full-page expansion is optional.
Middle School (5th–7th, ages 11–13): Full exercise—write 10, pick best 3, expand the winner to a paragraph AND a full page. Encourage them to experiment with tone (try one funny, one serious, one mysterious).
High School (9th+, age 15): Full exercise with the expansion going to a full page. Additionally, after expanding, look back at the 6-word original and reflect: what did the expanded version gain? What did it lose? Sometimes less really is more.

What Success Looks Like

  • Every child produces multiple six-word stories and can identify their best one
  • At least one story from each kid makes the family react (laugh, gasp, think)
  • Kids experience that constraints can spark creativity, not limit it
  • The expansion exercise proves that starting small can lead to something big
