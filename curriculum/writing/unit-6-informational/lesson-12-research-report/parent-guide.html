Lesson 12: The Research Report MULTI-SESSION

Unit 6: Informational Writing  |  Time: ~95 minutes across 3 sessions  |  Ages: 6–15 (all tiers)  |  Materials: Research Planner worksheet, Report Draft worksheet, books/internet for research, note-taking supplies
The Big Idea: Pick a topic you’re genuinely CURIOUS about. Research it. Write about it. The key: they choose the topic. Interest drives effort. A kid who is fascinated by deep-sea creatures will write a better report than a kid assigned to write about the water cycle. Let them choose.

Session 1: Research (30 Minutes)
1 Pick a Topic (5 min)
Each kid picks something they are genuinely curious about. Not “what sounds like a school topic” but “what do I actually want to know more about?” Dinosaurs, black holes, how video games are made, the history of chocolate, why cats purr — anything goes.
2 Find 3+ Sources (10 min)
Help kids find sources. Books, encyclopedias, websites, videos. Teach the reliability check:
  • Who wrote this? Are they an expert?
  • Is it from a trusted source (library book, .edu, National Geographic, etc.)?
  • Is it recent enough for this topic?
  • Does it match what other sources say?
3 Take Notes (15 min)
Using the Research Planner. The KEY skill: paraphrasing. Put it in YOUR words. If you can’t explain it without looking at the source, you don’t understand it yet.
Quoting vs. Paraphrasing vs. Plagiarizing:
  • Quoting: Using the exact words in quotation marks with the source named. “According to NASA, ‘Mars has the largest volcano in the solar system.’”
  • Paraphrasing: Putting the idea in your own words. “The biggest volcano anywhere in our solar system is on Mars (NASA).”
  • Plagiarizing: Copying someone’s words and pretending they’re yours. Don’t do this. Ever.
Session 2: Outline + Draft (45 Minutes)
1 Organize Notes into an Outline (15 min)
Using the Research Planner outline section. Structure:
  1. Introduction: Hook + thesis (what’s the main point of your report?)
  2. Body Section 1: First main topic with evidence from research
  3. Body Section 2: Second main topic
  4. Body Section 3: Third main topic (optional for younger kids)
  5. Conclusion: So what? Why does this matter? What’s the takeaway?
2 Write the Draft (30 min)
Using the Report Draft worksheet. Remind them to use their outline, not to start from scratch. Each section should have a topic sentence followed by evidence from their research. Encourage them to use techniques from earlier lessons: hooks (L3), strong openings and closings (L4).
Session 3: Revision (20 Minutes)
1 Self-Review with Checklist (8 min)
Use the revision checklist on the Report Draft worksheet. Read through once checking for content, once checking for clarity, once checking for citations.
2 Peer Review with a Sibling (7 min)
Swap reports. The reviewer marks: “This part was really interesting,” “I got confused here,” “I wanted to know more about this.”
3 Polish (5 min)
Make final revisions. Check spelling. Make sure sources are cited.

How to Evaluate Sources

More Reliable:
  • Library books
  • Encyclopedias
  • .edu and .gov websites
  • National Geographic, Smithsonian, BBC
  • Written by named experts
  • Cites its own sources
Less Reliable:
  • Random blogs with no author
  • Social media posts
  • Sites trying to sell something
  • Wikipedia (fine for overview, but check its sources)
  • Outdated information
  • Only one source says it

Simple Citation Format

Teach a simple format kids can use consistently:

  • Book: Author Last Name, First Name. Title. Publisher, Year.
  • Website: “Article Title.” Website Name, URL, Date Accessed.
  • Video: “Video Title.” Channel/Creator, Date Published.
Tip: For Elementary and below, a simple “I learned this from [Book Title] by [Author]” is sufficient. Middle School should use the format above. High School should use full MLA-style citations.

Age-Tier Adaptations

PRIMARY (Age 6, K–1)
“My Topic Report” — Pick a topic (an animal is a great choice). Learn 5 interesting facts. Draw pictures. Write one sentence per fact (with help). The “sources” are whatever books or videos you explored together. Total output: 5 illustrated sentences.
ELEMENTARY (Ages 9–10, 3rd–4th)
Writes a 1-page report with an introduction, 2–3 body paragraphs, and a conclusion. Uses at least 2 sources. Writes down where they found their information. Paraphrases (doesn’t copy).
MIDDLE SCHOOL (Ages 11–13, 5th–7th)
Writes a 2–3 page report with proper structure: hook, thesis, topic sentences, evidence from research, conclusion. Uses at least 3 sources with proper citations. Includes at least one direct quote.
HIGH SCHOOL (Age 15, 9th+)
Writes a 3–5 page thesis-driven report. The report should not just present facts but ARGUE a point or answer a question. Uses 4+ sources with a bibliography in MLA format. Demonstrates the difference between summarizing, analyzing, and arguing. Includes proper in-text citations.

What to Look For

  • Did they choose a topic they’re genuinely interested in?
  • Are the notes in their OWN words (not copied)?
  • Did they use multiple sources?
  • Does the report have a clear structure (not just a list of random facts)?
  • Is there a thesis or main idea that holds it together?
  • Does the conclusion say “so what” — why this topic matters?
  • Are sources cited?
