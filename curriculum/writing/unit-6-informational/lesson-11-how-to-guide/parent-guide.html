Lesson 11: The How-To Guide

Unit 6: Informational Writing  |  Time: 45 minutes (30 min write + 15 min sibling test)  |  Ages: 6–15 (all tiers)  |  Materials: Worksheet, whatever materials the kid needs to demonstrate their topic
The Big Idea: Write instructions for something YOU know how to do. Then give them to a sibling who DOESN’T know. Can they follow them? The moment someone fails to follow your instructions because they were unclear is hilarious and deeply educational. Good instructional writing requires EMPATHY — imagining you know NOTHING and writing for that person.

Lesson Flow (45 Minutes)

1 Read BAD Instructions — Laugh (3 min)
Read a deliberately terrible set of instructions aloud. Examples:
How to Make a Peanut Butter Sandwich:
1. Get bread.
2. Put peanut butter on it.
3. Done!
(How much bread? Where does the peanut butter go? What about the other slice? A knife? The lid?)
Ask: “What’s missing? What would go wrong if you followed these exactly?”
2 Read GOOD Instructions — Identify What Works (3 min)
Read a clear, well-written set of instructions. Notice:
What makes instructions clear:
  • Numbered steps in order
  • Precise language (“1 cup” not “some”)
  • Materials listed before you start
  • Anticipates questions (“If the dough is too sticky, add a little more flour”)
  • Describes what the result should look like
3 Pick a Topic and Write (20 min)
Each kid picks something THEY are genuinely good at. They use the How-To Planner worksheet to list materials, write numbered steps, and anticipate questions. Remind them: write as if the reader has never done this before.
4 THE SIBLING TEST (15 min)
This is the best part. Swap instructions with a sibling. The tester follows the instructions EXACTLY AS WRITTEN — no guessing, no filling in gaps. If step 3 says “put it in the oven” but doesn’t say what temperature, the tester stops and says “I don’t know what temperature.” This is where the learning happens. Let it be messy and funny.
5 Revise Based on What Happened (5 min)
Go back and fix the steps that caused confusion. This teaches revision through lived experience — far more powerful than any checklist.

Topic Ideas

Each kid should pick something THEY are genuinely good at:

How to make your favorite sandwich or recipe
How to draw a specific character
How to do a card trick or magic trick
How to beat a specific level in a game
How to braid hair or do a hairstyle
How to build something in Minecraft/Lego
How to care for a pet
How to make a paper airplane that flies
Tip — The Empathy Connection: The core skill here is perspective-taking. “What do I know that my reader doesn’t?” is the fundamental question of all clear writing. When a sibling fails to follow a step, that’s not the sibling’s fault — it’s the writer’s. This is a powerful and memorable lesson.

Age-Tier Adaptations

PRIMARY (Age 6, K–1)
Does 3–5 simple steps. Draws a picture for each step (like a picture recipe). Parent helps write the words. The sibling test still works beautifully at this age — a 6-year-old’s instructions for making a sandwich will produce hilarious results.
ELEMENTARY (Ages 9–10, 3rd–4th)
Writes 5–8 clear, numbered steps. Includes a materials list. Does the sibling test and records where confusion happened.
MIDDLE SCHOOL (Ages 11–13, 5th–7th)
Writes the full guide: materials, 8–15 numbered steps, tips/warnings, troubleshooting section (“If X happens, do Y”). Does the sibling test and revises based on results.
HIGH SCHOOL (Age 15, 9th+)
Writes the full guide with a troubleshooting section, common mistakes, and a description of the finished result. Does the sibling test, revises, and produces a polished final version that could be posted online or given to a real beginner.

What to Look For

  • Are the steps in logical order?
  • Is the language specific enough? (“1 tablespoon” vs. “some”)
  • Are materials listed before the steps begin?
  • Could a total beginner follow these without asking questions?
  • Did the sibling test reveal gaps? Were those gaps fixed?
  • Is there a description of what success looks like?
