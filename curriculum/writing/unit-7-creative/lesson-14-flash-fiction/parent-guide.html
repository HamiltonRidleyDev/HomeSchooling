Lesson 14: Flash Fiction

A Complete Story in 500 Words — Unit 7: Creative Writing — Parent Guide

Duration: Session 1: 30 min • Session 2: 25 min
Materials: This guide, Story Planner, Story Draft worksheet
Ages: All tiers (6–15)
Curriculum: Writing

Lesson Overview

Flash fiction is a complete short story — characters, conflict, resolution — in 500 words or less. The constraint is the teacher. When you only have 500 words, every single one matters. This lesson builds the editing muscle like nothing else because students can't hide behind length. Every sentence has to earn its place.

This is a two-session lesson: Session 1 is planning and drafting, Session 2 is peer review and revision.

The Core Idea

Tell them: "You know how some people write ten pages and say nothing? Today you're going to write one page and say EVERYTHING. A complete story — beginning, middle, end — in 500 words. If a sentence doesn't earn its place, it's gone."

Flash Fiction Rules of Thumb
  • ONE character, ONE problem, ONE scene. Keep it focused. You don't have room for subplots.
  • Start as LATE as possible in the story. Skip the backstory. Drop us into the moment.
  • End as EARLY as possible. Once the turning point lands, get out. Don't over-explain.
  • Use dialogue to move fast. Dialogue advances plot AND reveals character simultaneously.
  • Every sentence must do at least 2 jobs — advance plot AND reveal character, or set mood AND build tension. Single-job sentences get cut.
Session 1 — Plan + Draft (30 min)
Step 1 — Read a Flash Fiction Piece Together (2 min)

Read the mentor flash fiction below aloud. Then ask: "That was a complete story. What did you notice? How did they fit so much into so few words?"

"The Last Garden" — Mentor Flash Fiction

The eviction notice said thirty days, but Maria started digging that afternoon.

Not packing. Digging.

"Mom, what are you doing?" Alejandro stood at the back door, backpack still on.

"Planting tomatoes."

"We're leaving in a month."

"Tomatoes take six weeks." She pressed a seed into the dark soil and covered it like a secret. "Someone will live here after us. They'll find tomatoes."

Alejandro dropped his backpack on the porch and knelt beside her. The soil was cold. His knees were going to be filthy. He didn't care.

"What else are we planting?"

She handed him a packet of sunflower seeds without looking up, but he caught the edge of her smile.

They worked until the streetlights came on. Thirty days wasn't long enough to grow roots, but Maria had never let that stop her before.

— Original example for classroom use (148 words)

Discussion points: Notice how it starts with action, not backstory. The dialogue does double duty — it moves the plot AND shows us who Maria is. The ending doesn't over-explain. "Thirty days wasn't long enough to grow roots" works on two levels — literal and figurative.

Step 2 — Plan Using the Story Planner (8 min)

Hand out the Story Planner worksheet. Walk through each section briefly. The key constraint: ONE character, ONE problem, ONE scene. If they're planning a multi-chapter epic, gently redirect.

The "Map your story in 5 sentences" section is critical — if they can tell the story in 5 sentences, they can write it in 500 words.

Step 3 — Draft (20 min)

Using the Story Draft worksheet, write the full story. Target: 400–600 words. They'll cut to 500 in revision. Remind them:

  • Start in the middle of the action (use a hook from Lesson 3)
  • Use dialogue — it moves fast and sounds natural
  • Don't wrap up with a moral or lesson — let the story speak
Session 2 — Peer Review + Revise (25 min)
Step 4 — Peer Review with a Sibling (10 min)

Pair up siblings. Each reads the other's story and fills in the Peer Review section on the draft worksheet. The three questions are simple but powerful:

  1. One thing that worked — be specific ("the dialogue felt real" not "it was good")
  2. One thing I wanted more of — this helps the writer see what's missing
  3. One question I had — this reveals where the story is unclear

Use the shared Peer Feedback Guide if needed for structure.

Step 5 — Revise and Tighten (15 min)

Now the real work: cut the story to 500 words or less. This is where the lesson hits hardest. They'll need to:

  • Cut any sentence that only does one job
  • Replace weak verbs with strong ones (Lesson 1)
  • Delete backstory the reader doesn't need
  • Tighten dialogue — cut tags where the speaker is obvious
  • Apply the 20% cut from Lesson 5

Track word count before and after. The number of words cut is a badge of honor.

Common Struggles and Solutions
  • "I can't fit it in 500 words!" — Good. That's the point. Ask: "What can you cut without losing the story?" Usually the answer is the beginning.
  • "My story doesn't have enough conflict." — Ask: "What does your character WANT? What's stopping them?" Every story needs that tension.
  • "I don't know how to end it." — End right after the turning point. The reader can fill in the rest. Endings that trust the reader are the strongest.
  • "It's too short." — Add a specific sensory detail. Add one line of dialogue that reveals character. Don't pad — deepen.

Age-Tier Adjustments

Primary (Age 6): Draws a 4-panel comic strip telling a complete story. Each panel has a picture, a speech bubble, and a caption sentence. The 4 panels = setup, problem, turning point, resolution. Help them write the captions.
Elementary (Ages 9–10): Writes a 200-word story using the simplified planner. Focus on having a clear beginning, middle, and end. The word limit is a soft target, not a hard constraint.
Middle School (Ages 11–13): Writes a 300–400 word story. Uses the full planner. Does the peer review and revision. Track word count.
High School (Age 15): Writes exactly 500 words — not 499, not 501. The constraint IS the challenge. Full planner, peer review, revision. Push for literary techniques: imagery, subtext, an ending that resonates.

Materials Needed

  • Story Planner worksheet (1 per student)
  • Story Draft worksheet (1 per student)
  • Pencils/pens + erasers (revision is physical today)
  • Optional: colored pencils for the Primary comic strip
  • Timer for each session segment
