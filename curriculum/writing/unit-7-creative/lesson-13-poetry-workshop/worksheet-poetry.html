Section 1: Haiku

Haiku Rules
  • Line 1: 5 syllables
  • Line 2: 7 syllables
  • Line 3: 5 syllables

Usually about nature or a single moment frozen in time. The power of haiku is what it leaves out.

Example
An old silent pond (5) A frog jumps into the pond (7) Splash! Silence again. (5)

— Matsuo Basho

Syllable Tip

Clap it out! Each clap = one syllable. "Wa-ter-mel-on" = 4 claps = 4 syllables.

Section 2: Acrostic

Acrostic Rules

Write a word vertically down the page. Each letter starts a line that relates to that word. Don't just list adjectives — tell tiny stories. Make each line a surprise.

Example: BRAVE
B — Beyond the fear that grips my chest R — Rising when my legs say stop A — Always one more step to take V — Victory isn't the absence of fear E — Every hero shakes before they leap

Section 3: Free Verse Elementary +

Free Verse Rules

No rules about rhyme, syllables, or length. Just make it REAL. Write about something you feel deeply. Be specific. Be honest.

Pick a prompt (or use your own):
  • "Home is..."
  • "I am from..."
  • "What I want you to know about me is..."
  • "When no one is watching, I..."
  • Your own topic:

Section 4: Spoken Word Middle + High School

Spoken Word Rules

Written to be performed OUT LOUD. Use repetition, rhythm, emotion. It should sound powerful. This isn't quiet poetry — this is poetry with a heartbeat.

Write about:
  • Something you believe in
  • Something that makes you angry
  • Something you wish people understood
  • Something you're tired of hearing

Family Poetry Slam

Slam Rules
  • Stand up (or sit up tall)
  • Read like you mean it
  • Applause after every single poem — no exceptions
  • Scoring is for fun, not stress

Age-Tier Guide

Primary (Age 6): Shape poem + simple acrostic with drawing. Performs shape poem at the slam.
Elementary (Ages 9–10): Haiku (3) + Acrostic (1) + Free Verse (1). Performs at the slam.
