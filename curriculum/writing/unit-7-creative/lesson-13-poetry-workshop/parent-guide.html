Lesson 13: Poetry Workshop

Unit 7: Creative Writing — Parent Guide

Duration: 40 minutes
Materials: This guide, Poetry worksheet
Ages: All tiers (6–15)
Curriculum: Writing

Lesson Overview

This is a poetry workshop, not a poetry lecture. The goal is to make poets out of everyone in 40 minutes by cycling through four forms: Haiku, Acrostic, Free Verse, and Spoken Word. Each form gets the same treatment: read examples, talk about what makes them work, then write. The lesson ends with a family poetry slam because poems are meant to be heard.

The #1 Rule for This Lesson

Do NOT force rhyming. Rhyming almost always makes kid poetry worse, not better. It forces them to choose words for sound instead of meaning, and the results are sing-songy and hollow. Free them from the rhyming trap and watch what happens. Feelings over cleverness. Specific over vague. Every time.

Lesson Flow

Step 1 — Opening Frame (2 min)

Say this (or something like it): "Poetry isn't about rhyming — it's about making someone FEEL something in as few words as possible. A poem can be three lines. It can be a hundred lines. The only rule is: make it real. Today we're going to write a LOT of poems, fast. Don't overthink. Just feel."

Step 2 — Haiku: 5/7/5 (8 min)

Teach: Haiku is a Japanese form — three lines, 5/7/5 syllables. Traditionally about nature or a single moment frozen in time. The power of haiku is what it leaves OUT.

Read aloud: Share the mentor haiku below. Clap out the syllables together.

Write: Everyone writes 3 haiku. Set a timer — keep it moving.

Mentor Haiku

Classic Haiku
An old silent pond A frog jumps into the pond Splash! Silence again.
— Matsuo Basho (translated)
The light of a candle Is transferred to another candle — Spring twilight.
— Yosa Buson (translated)
Modern Haiku
I write, erase, rewrite Erase again, and then A poppy blooms.
— Katsushika Hokushi (translated)
First day of school — my lunchbox heavy with things to say.
— Modern student haiku
Dog at the window nose prints on the cold, cold glass — waiting for my car.
— Modern family haiku
Step 3 — Acrostic: Your Name as a Poem (8 min)

Teach: Write a word vertically down the page. Each letter begins a line that relates to that word. The trick: don't just list adjectives. Tell tiny stories. Make each line a surprise.

Read aloud: Share the mentor acrostic below.

Write: Everyone writes 1 acrostic using their name OR a word they love.

Mentor Acrostic

BRAVE
B — Beyond the fear that grips my chest R — Rising when my legs say stop A — Always one more step to take V — Victory isn't the absence of fear E — Every hero shakes before they leap
HOME
H — Hot chocolate on a Tuesday for no reason O — Open doors that never lock M — Mom humming a song she forgot the words to E — Everyone together, even when we're quiet
RAIN
R — Rhythms on the roof like a thousand tiny drums A — After the storm, the world smells brand new I — I stand at the window and watch it all come down N — Nothing sounds like rain when you're safe inside
Step 4 — Free Verse: No Rules Except Make It Real (10 min)

Teach: Free verse has no rules about rhyme, syllables, or length. The only rule: it has to make the reader feel something. The best free verse poems are specific — not "I love nature" but "the way moss covers the north side of the oak tree behind Grandma's house."

Read aloud: Share the mentor poems below. Read them slowly. Let them land.

Write: Everyone writes 1 free verse poem. Give prompts for those who need them: "Home is..." / "I am from..." / "What I want you to know about me is..."

Mentor Free Verse

From "Dreams" by Langston Hughes
Hold fast to dreams For if dreams die Life is a broken-winged bird That cannot fly. Hold fast to dreams For when dreams go Life is a barren field Frozen with snow.
— Langston Hughes
From "The Summer Day" by Mary Oliver
I don't know exactly what a prayer is. I do know how to pay attention, how to fall down into the grass, how to kneel down in the grass, how to be idle and blessed, how to stroll through the fields, which is what I have been doing all day. Tell me, what else should I have done? Doesn't everything die at last, and too soon? Tell me, what is it you plan to do with your one wild and precious life?
— Mary Oliver
From "Listen to the Mustn'ts" by Shel Silverstein
Listen to the MUSTN'TS, child, Listen to the DON'TS Listen to the SHOULDN'TS The IMPOSSIBLES, the WON'TS Listen to the NEVER HAVES Then listen close to me — Anything can happen, child, ANYTHING can be.
— Shel Silverstein
Step 5 — Spoken Word: Poetry That Performs (8 min)

Teach: Spoken word is poetry written to be performed OUT LOUD. It uses repetition, rhythm, pauses, volume changes, and raw emotion. It should sound powerful. It's not just about what you say — it's about how you say it.

Read aloud (or perform!): Share the example below. Read it with energy, pauses, and emphasis. Show them what performance looks like.

Write: Everyone writes a short spoken word piece about something they believe in, something that frustrates them, or something they wish people understood.

Mentor Spoken Word

"What I Know" — Example Spoken Word
I know what it feels like to be the smallest one in the room. I know what it feels like when no one asks your opinion because they already decided you don't have one. I know the sound of being talked over. I know the weight of swallowing words you spent all night rehearsing. But I also know this: My voice doesn't need to be the loudest to be the truest. My words don't need to fill the room to change it. So the next time you forget to listen, I won't get louder. I'll just get more honest. And honest is harder to ignore.
— Original example for classroom use
"Small" — Example Spoken Word
They say "think big" but nobody ever talks about the power of thinking small. Small like: remembering someone's favorite color. Small like: holding the door for the person behind you. Small like: saying "I'm sorry" first. Because big changes? They don't start with big people doing big things. They start with small people doing small things so many times that the world can't ignore them anymore.
— Original example for classroom use
Step 6 — Family Poetry Slam! (5 min)

Everyone picks their best poem from the session and performs it for the family. Rules:

  • Stand up (or at least sit up tall)
  • Read it like you mean it
  • Eye contact at least once
  • Applause is required after every poem
  • Family votes on favorites (for fun, NOT stress)

Use the scoring table on the worksheet — keep it lighthearted and encouraging.

Teaching Tips
  • Feelings over cleverness. A poem that makes you feel something real beats a poem with fancy wordplay every time.
  • Specific over vague. "The scratchy wool blanket on Grandpa's couch" is better than "warm memories."
  • Don't force rhyming. If someone naturally rhymes, great. But never let rhyme drive word choice. "I was sad / it was bad" teaches nothing.
  • Read poems aloud. Always. Poetry is sound as much as meaning.
  • Model vulnerability. Write your own poem during the lesson. Share it at the slam. Show them it's safe to be honest.

Age-Tier Adjustments

Primary (Age 6): Does shape poems (write words in the shape of the object — a poem about a tree in the shape of a tree) + simple acrostic using their name. Can draw illustrations alongside. At the slam, they read or recite their shape poem.
Elementary (Ages 9–10): Haiku (write 3) + Acrostic (write 1) + Free Verse (write 1). They can skip the spoken word writing but should still listen to the examples. Perform at the slam.
Middle School (Ages 11–13): All four forms. Full participation in the slam. Encourage them to take risks with the spoken word piece.
High School (Age 15): All four forms + performs at the slam with genuine performance effort (pacing, emphasis, eye contact). Push for depth and honesty in the free verse and spoken word pieces.

Materials Needed

  • Poetry worksheet (1 per student)
  • Pencils/pens
  • Timer (for keeping sections moving)
  • Optional: printed copies of the mentor poems for kids to follow along
