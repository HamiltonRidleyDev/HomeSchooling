The Grocery Store Challenge

Parent Guide & Facilitator Notes

Unit 3: Smart Spending — Lesson 5 (Field Trip)

What is this? A real-world field trip lesson where each kid gets real cash, a real meal assignment, and has to plan, shop, and stay within budget at an actual grocery store. The family eats what they buy. Real stakes, real learning.

Time: 45–60 minutes total (10 min briefing + 25-35 min store + 5 min checkout + 10 min debrief)
Location: Your home + a grocery store
Materials: Cash (see prep checklist), printed worksheets, pencils, clipboards (one per kid), calculator (ages 9+)

Lesson Flow

1
Pre-Trip Briefing — 10 min at home
Hand out assignments and cash. Go over each kid's worksheet. Explain the rules:
  • Stay in budget. The cash in your hand is ALL you get. No asking for more.
  • Compare prices. Always look at store brand vs. name brand.
  • Check unit prices. The little label on the shelf edge tells you price per ounce/unit.
  • Fill out your worksheet as you go. Write down prices before putting items in the cart.
  • The family eats what you buy. Choose wisely!
Let each kid read their assignment out loud so everyone knows what they're responsible for.
2
At the Store — 25-35 min
Kids shop using their worksheets on clipboards. They find items, compare prices, write down costs, and track their running total.
  • Primary (age 6): Stays with parent the entire time. Parent helps read labels but lets the child make choices.
  • Elementary (ages 9-10): Can browse nearby aisles independently. Check in every 5-10 minutes.
  • Middle School (ages 11-13): Shop independently within the store. Meet at checkout at the agreed time.
  • High School (age 15): Fully independent. Set a meeting time at checkout.
Set a timer — announce "10 minutes left!" and "5 minutes — head to checkout!"
3
Checkout Reality — 5 min
Each kid checks out separately (younger ones with parent handling the transaction). This is the moment of truth — does the register total match their worksheet? Are they under budget?

If someone is over budget: They put something back. Right there at the register. This is not a punishment — this is the lesson. Every adult has had to put something back. It's a real skill.
4
Post-Trip Debrief — 10 min at home
Back at home, everyone fills out the reflection sheet. Then discuss together:
  • Who spent the most per serving? Who spent the least?
  • Who found the best store-brand deal?
  • What surprised you about prices?
  • What would you do differently next time?
  • How does this compare to the Price Catalog from Build Your Life?
Celebrate everyone. The 6-year-old who bought PB&J ingredients deserves the same recognition as the 15-year-old who planned a full day of meals.

Age Assignments

Primary Age 6 $8 cash PB&J + fruit snack

Worksheet: shopping-worksheet-primary.html

Assignment: Buy ingredients for PB&J sandwiches and a fruit snack for the family.

  • Items: bread, peanut butter, jelly, fruit (bananas or apples)
  • Parent accompanies the entire time
  • Simple price comparison: find the cheapest bread (store brand vs. name brand)
  • Practice: counting money, reading price tags, making a choice

Elementary Ages 9-10 $20 cash One dinner for 6

Worksheet: shopping-worksheet-elementary.html

Assignment: Buy ingredients for ONE complete dinner for the family (6 people).

  • Must include: a protein, a starch/grain, and a vegetable
  • Find one item where store brand is cheaper — record the savings
  • Track a running total on their worksheet
  • Practice: meal planning, addition, price comparison

Middle School Ages 11-13 $35 cash Two dinners for 6

Worksheet: shopping-worksheet-middle.html

Assignment: Buy ingredients for TWO different dinners for the family.

  • Must include variety — can't make the same meal twice, must use two different proteins
  • Track unit prices and calculate cost per serving
  • Find 3 items where store brand saves money
  • Compare two sizes of the same product using unit price
  • Practice: meal planning, unit price math, cost-per-serving analysis

High School Age 15 $50 cash Full day of meals for 6

Worksheet: shopping-worksheet-highschool.html

Assignment: Buy ingredients for a full day — breakfast, lunch, and dinner — for the family.

  • Must include reasonable nutritional balance across all meals
  • Track cost per serving per meal
  • Analyze unit prices on 3+ items
  • Compare total cost to estimated restaurant equivalent
  • Practice: full meal planning, budgeting across categories, real-world cost analysis

Prep Checklist

Complete the night before or morning of the trip:

  • Get cash in the right denominations:
    • Age 6: $8 — give as 1 five + 3 ones (easy to count)
    • Ages 9-10: $20 — give as 1 ten + 2 fives (or a $20 bill)
    • Ages 11-13: $35 — give as 1 twenty + 1 ten + 1 five
    • Age 15: $50 — give as 2 twenties + 1 ten
    • Total cash needed: $113 (if all 6 kids participate — adjust if combining Elementary kids on one assignment)
  • Print worksheets — one per kid at their level, plus the reflection sheet for everyone
  • Gather clipboards — one per kid (or use a hardcover book)
  • Pencils — one per kid, plus a spare
  • Calculators — for ages 9+ (phone calculators work fine)
  • Reusable grocery bags — one per kid helps keep their items separate
  • Pick your store — a store with clear shelf labels and unit pricing works best. Avoid warehouse clubs (bulk sizes make budgets tricky for younger kids).
  • Know the store layout — if possible, know where produce, bread, meat, and dairy are so you can give directions
  • Account for tax — in most states, groceries are not taxed, but check your state. If taxed, mention this in the briefing and have kids leave a small buffer.

Safety Notes

Store Safety Plan:
  • Age 6: Always with a parent. No exceptions.
  • Ages 9-10: Stay within 1-2 aisles of a parent. Establish a "home base" aisle if they need help.
  • Ages 11-13: Can move freely in the store. Know the parent's phone number. Check in at the agreed time.
  • Age 15: Fully independent. Has a phone or knows where to meet.
  • Establish a meeting point before you split up (e.g., "Meet at the front registers at 3:45").
  • Rule for everyone: If you need help, find a store employee or come to the meeting point.
  • No leaving the store for any reason without a parent.

Tips for Making It Fun (Not Stressful)

Frame it as a challenge, not a test. Use language like "Let's see who can find the best deals" rather than "Don't mess this up."
  • Build excitement: "Tonight, YOU are in charge of dinner. The whole family is counting on you."
  • No judgment on food choices — if the 9-year-old wants to make hot dogs and mac & cheese, that's a valid dinner. The goal is budgeting, not gourmet cooking.
  • Celebrate the store-brand discoveries. When a kid finds that store brand cereal is $1.50 cheaper, make a big deal of it.
  • Let them struggle a little. If they're staring at the shelf confused, give them a minute before jumping in. Problem-solving IS the lesson.
  • Don't rescue them at checkout. If they're $2 over, they put something back. Sympathize ("That's tough, isn't it?") but don't bail them out.
  • Praise the process: "I saw you comparing two brands — that's exactly what smart shoppers do."
  • After the trip, actually cook and eat what they bought. This is the payoff. "You planned this meal and bought the ingredients — now look at what we're eating!"

What If Someone Goes Over Budget?

This is not a failure — this is the most valuable part of the lesson.

If a kid's total at checkout exceeds their budget:

  1. Stay calm and supportive. "Looks like we're a little over. That happens to adults too. Let's figure out what to put back."
  2. Let THEM decide what to remove. Don't choose for them. Ask: "Which item could you live without?"
  3. The cashier will understand. This happens all the time. You can say, "We're doing a budgeting lesson — they need to put one thing back."
  4. After checkout, talk about it: "What could you have done differently to stay under?" (Checked the running total more carefully, picked the store brand, chosen a different protein, etc.)
  5. On the reflection sheet, have them write what they'd change. This cements the lesson.
