Unit 3 · Smart Spending · Lesson 6

Death by a Thousand Cuts

The Latte Factor — How Small Purchases Drain Big Wealth
Format: Split Lesson (2 sessions + tracking week)
Session 1: 15 min
Gap: 1 week of tracking
Session 2: 20 min
Ages: 6–15 (all tiers)

The Big Idea

Small, daily purchases feel harmless in the moment, but they compound into enormous amounts over time. A $6 coffee every workday costs $1,560 a year. A $15 DoorDash order three times a week costs $2,340 a year. And if you invested that money instead at 10% annual returns, it would grow to over $100,000 in 20 years.

The "surprise moment" in this lesson comes when your family tallies up a real week of small purchases and sees the total projected out over years. This is not about deprivation — it is about awareness. Some small purchases are absolutely worth it. But most of us have no idea how much we are actually spending on autopilot.

Materials Needed

Reference Sheet: Common Small Purchases
Weekly Tracker: 1 per family member
Calculation Worksheet: 1 per participant
Pencils/pens for each tracker
Calculator (phone is fine)
Clipboards or folders to carry trackers
Session 1 — The Hook
15 minutes · Introduction & tracker handout
1The $6-to-$100K Chain
5 min
Say This
"I'm going to show you how $6 can become $100,000. Ready?"

Write the math chain on a whiteboard, big paper, or just say it out loud while everyone watches:

  • $6 per day (one coffee shop latte)
  • $42 per week (7 days)
  • $182 per month
  • $2,184 per year
  • $21,840 in 10 years (just the cash!)
  • $139,000+ invested over 30 years at 10% annual returns

Pause after the last number. Let the shock register. Ask: "From SIX dollars a day?"

Tip
For younger kids, simplify: "If I gave you $6 every single day for a whole year, you'd have over two thousand dollars. That's enough to buy [something they want]."
2Walk Through the Reference Sheet
5 min

Hand out the Common Small Purchases reference sheet. Read through the items together as a family.

For each row, ask: "Does our family do this? How often?"

Point out the "Invested 20yr" column. Let the kids react to the big green numbers. Don't lecture — the numbers do the talking.

Say This
"Nobody is saying you can never buy coffee or eat out. The question is: do you KNOW how much it adds up to? Most people have no idea. This week, we're going to find out."
3Hand Out Trackers
3 min

Give each family member a Weekly Tracking Sheet. Explain the rules:

  • Write down every small purchase anyone in the family notices — coffee, snacks, vending machines, impulse Amazon buys, app purchases, fast food, convenience store runs.
  • Include the cost and whether it was a need or a want.
  • Carry your tracker everywhere for the next 7 days.
  • No judgment — we are just observing, like scientists collecting data.
Important
This works MUCH better if parents track too. If the kids see that Mom and Dad are writing down their own coffee purchases and impulse buys, it becomes a family project instead of a lecture. Be honest about your own habits.
4Set the Challenge
2 min
Say This
"In one week, we are going to add up everything. I want to see how much our family really spends on small stuff. My prediction: it's going to be way more than any of us think. Let's find out who's right."

Decide where trackers will live (on the fridge, in backpacks, etc.) and set a daily check-in time if helpful.

📋

Tracking Week — 7 Days

Everyone carries their tracker. Write down every small purchase.
Check in briefly each evening: "Did you write down everything today?"
No shaming. No restricting. Just observe and record.

Session 2 — The Big Reveal
20 minutes · Calculation & debrief
1Collect and Total
5 min

Everyone brings their completed tracker to the table. Each person totals their week if they have not already.

Go around the table — each person shares:

  • Their weekly total
  • How many purchases they made
  • Their biggest single purchase
  • Their most common purchase

Then calculate the family total for the week. Write it big where everyone can see.

2Fill Out the Calculation Worksheet
5 min

Hand out the Calculation Worksheet. Walk through each section together:

  • Section 1: Take the weekly total and multiply out to monthly, yearly, 5 years, 10 years.
  • Section 2: Use the lookup tables to see what that money could become if invested.
Tip
Younger kids can use a calculator or have an older sibling help with the math. The point is seeing the big number, not doing long multiplication by hand.
3The Big Reveal
3 min
Say This
"Our family spent $____ on small purchases this week. That's $____ per year. If we invested that instead, it would become $____ in 20 years."

Fill in the real numbers. Let everyone react. This is usually the moment where jaws drop.

Keep It Positive
Don't make anyone feel guilty. The point is awareness, not shame. Say: "This isn't about never spending money on things we enjoy. It's about making sure we're spending on purpose, not on autopilot."
4Worth It or Mindless?
4 min

Go back through the trackers together. For each purchase (or category), ask:

Discussion Questions
  • "Which of these purchases were WORTH it — brought you real joy or filled a real need?"
  • "Which were mindless — you barely remember them or wouldn't miss them?"
  • "Which were habit purchases — things you buy automatically without thinking?"
  • "What could you have done instead for free or cheaper?"
5The Cut List Challenge
3 min

Each person fills out Section 3 of the Calculation Worksheet: pick 1–3 small expenses they would be willing to eliminate or reduce.

They calculate the monthly savings, annual savings, and what it would grow to if invested over 20 years.

Then Section 4: "What would you DO with that money instead?" Let each person share their answer.

Make It Concrete
Help them connect the savings to something real. "Giving up vending machine snacks saves $528 a year — that's a new gaming console." Or "Cooking instead of DoorDash 3 times a week saves $2,340 — that's a family vacation."

Age Differentiation

Primary (Age 6)

Tracker: Uses the simplified version — tally marks for "how many small things did we buy today?" with a number line to circle. No dollar amounts needed.

Debrief: Count total tallies for the week. Ask: "Was that a lot or a little?" Simple counting and "what would you buy with $___?"

Elementary (Ages 8–10)

Tracker: Full tracker with dollar amounts. May need help looking up prices.

Debrief: Basic multiplication (weekly x 4, weekly x 52). Use a calculator. Focus on the yearly number.

Middle School (Ages 11–13)

Tracker: Full tracker independently. Encouraged to also track digital purchases (in-app, subscriptions).

Debrief: Complete the full worksheet including the lookup tables for investment growth.

High School (Age 15)

Tracker: Full tracker plus categorizing purchases (food, entertainment, convenience, digital).

Debrief: Full worksheet with investment calculations. Challenge: calculate exact compound interest using the formula or a compound interest calculator online.

Family Participation Tips

Everyone Tracks, Not Just Kids

This lesson has the biggest impact when parents participate honestly. Kids are watching to see if you hold yourself to the same standard. Track your own coffee runs, impulse Amazon orders, and convenience purchases. When you share your total alongside theirs, it builds trust and makes the lesson stick.

  • Make it a game, not a punishment. See who can spot the most purchases during the week. Celebrate good tracking, not low spending.
  • Evening check-ins help. Spend 30 seconds at dinner asking "Did everyone write down their purchases today?" This keeps the trackers from getting forgotten.
  • Include everything. Grocery store impulse grabs, gas station candy, "it was only $3" purchases. Those are the whole point.
  • No shaming during the week. If someone buys a $7 smoothie, they write it down. That is all. Discussion happens in Session 2.

Connection to Build Your Life

Build Your Life Link

In the Build Your Life game (Lesson 1), each family set up a monthly budget for their household. Remind the kids: "Remember how tight the budget got when surprises hit? Small purchases are one of the biggest reasons real budgets fall apart — they don't show up as a single big line item, but they add up to hundreds or thousands a year."

If you kept the Build Your Life budgets, look back at them: "Where would these small purchases have fit? Would they have blown your budget?"

The money saved by cutting mindless small purchases is money that can go toward the goals they set in Build Your Life — saving for a car, building an emergency fund, or investing for the future.
