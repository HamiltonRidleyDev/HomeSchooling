Unit 5 — Growing Wealth

Stock Market Basics

Reference Sheet — What You're Actually Buying
For Everyone (All Ages)

What Is a Stock?

  • You buy a tiny piece of a company. You are now a part-owner.
  • If the company grows and makes money, your piece becomes worth more.
  • You can sell your piece anytime for whatever it's worth that day.
  • A "share" is just another word for one piece of stock.
Analogy: The Pizza
Imagine a pizza with 1,000 slices. You buy 1 slice for $10. The pizza shop gets really popular. Now people will pay $15 for a slice. Your $10 slice is worth $15! You didn't do anything — the company grew.

What Is a Stock Exchange?

  • A marketplace where stocks are bought and sold — like eBay for company ownership.
  • NYSE (New York Stock Exchange) and NASDAQ are the two biggest in the US.
  • Thousands of companies are "listed" — you can buy pieces of any of them.
  • The stock exchange is open Monday through Friday, 9:30 AM to 4:00 PM Eastern time.
Analogy: The Farmers' Market
Think of a farmers' market where every booth sells pieces of a different company instead of fruits and vegetables. You walk around, check the prices, and decide which "pieces" you want to buy. The next time you visit, the prices may have changed.

Why Do Stock Prices Go Up and Down?

  • Supply and demand: Lots of people want to buy a stock → price goes up. Lots of people want to sell → price goes down.
  • Good news (strong sales, exciting new product, growing fast) → price usually goes up.
  • Bad news (scandal, lost money, bad economy, product failure) → price usually goes down.
Key Insight: Short-term, prices bounce around daily — sometimes wildly. Long-term, good companies tend to grow. This is why investing is a patience game.
Analogy: The Sneaker Drop
When a hot new sneaker comes out and everyone wants it, the resale price shoots up. When a shoe isn't popular, the price drops. Stocks work the same way — the price reflects how badly people want to own a piece of that company.
For Ages 9+ (Elementary & Up)

What Is an Index Fund?

  • Instead of picking ONE company, you buy a piece of ALL of them (or the 500 biggest ones).
  • The S&P 500 index fund owns pieces of Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Nike, Disney, and 494 others.
  • If some companies go down, others go up — you're diversified (your eggs are in many baskets).
  • Over the last 100 years, the S&P 500 has averaged about 10% growth per year.
Warren Buffett's Advice: Warren Buffett — one of the richest investors in history — says most people should just buy index funds. He even bet $1 million that an S&P 500 index fund would beat professional stock pickers over 10 years. He won.
Analogy: The Fruit Basket
Picking one stock is like putting all your money on one apple. If that apple goes bad, you've lost everything. An index fund is like buying a basket of 500 different fruits. If one goes bad? No big deal — you still have 499 others.

Individual Stocks vs. Index Funds

Index Fund Individual Stock
Risk Lower (spread across 500 companies) Higher (one company)
Reward Steady growth (~10%/year average) Could be huge... or zero
Effort None (set it and forget it) High (must research constantly)
Best For Everyone, especially beginners People who enjoy research + accept risk
Fun Fact Beats ~90% of professional stock pickers over time Even experts often pick wrong
The Takeaway: Most professionals who try to beat the index fund can't do it consistently. If the pros can't do it, why would we try? Start with index funds, and only pick individual stocks with money you can afford to lose — and only if you enjoy the research.
For Ages 13+ (Middle School & Up)

How to Research a Company

Before buying stock in any company, smart investors ask these questions:

  • What do they sell? — Products, services, or both? Who are their customers?
  • Are they growing? — Is their revenue (total money coming in) going up year over year?
  • Do they make money? — Revenue is different from profit. Are they actually keeping more than they spend? (This is called "earnings.")
  • Who are their competitors? — Every company has rivals. How does this one stack up?
  • What's their "moat"? — A moat is what makes a company hard to compete with. Apple's moat is its ecosystem (iPhone + Mac + iPad + Watch all work together). Disney's moat is its characters and stories that people love for generations.
  • What could go wrong? — Every investment has risks. Government regulation? New competitor? Technology changing? Honest investors think about what could go wrong, not just what could go right.

Key Vocabulary

Stock / Share
A piece of ownership in a company. Owning 1 share = owning 1 tiny piece.
Ticker Symbol
The short code for a stock. AAPL = Apple, DIS = Disney, NKE = Nike, RBLX = Roblox.
Market Cap (Market Capitalization)
Total value of all shares combined. Tells you how "big" a company is. Apple's market cap is over $3 trillion.
Dividend
Some companies pay you cash just for owning their stock — like a thank-you payment, usually every 3 months.
Bull Market
When stocks are going UP overall. Called "bull" because a bull attacks by thrusting its horns upward.
Bear Market
When stocks are going DOWN overall. Called "bear" because a bear attacks by swiping its paws downward.
Portfolio
Your collection of all investments — stocks, index funds, bonds, etc. Like a folder holding all your financial stuff.
Diversification
Spreading your money across many different investments to reduce risk. "Don't put all your eggs in one basket."
IPO (Initial Public Offering)
When a company sells stock to the public for the first time. "Going public." Before an IPO, you can't buy shares.
Broker
A company or app that lets you buy and sell stocks. Examples: Fidelity, Charles Schwab, Robinhood.
S&P 500
An index tracking the 500 biggest public companies in the US. The most popular benchmark for "how the market is doing."
Index Fund
An investment that automatically buys all the stocks in an index (like the S&P 500). Low cost, low effort, historically strong returns.
