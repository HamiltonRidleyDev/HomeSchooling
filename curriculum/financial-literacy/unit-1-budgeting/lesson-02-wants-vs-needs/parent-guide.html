Wants vs. Needs — The Hard Choices Game

Parent Guide & Facilitator Notes

What is this? A card-sorting game where kids debate whether everyday items are NEEDS, WANTS, or somewhere in between. The core insight: it depends on your situation. A car is a luxury in Manhattan but a lifeline in rural Texas. After sorting, kids rank their wants and discover what they truly value.

Time: 25–30 minutes (can stretch to 40 if discussion is rich)
Players: 6 kids, ages 6–15
Materials: Printed item cards (cut apart), situation cards, sorting mat, ranking worksheets, pencils
Prep time: 10–15 minutes to print and cut cards

Core Concept

There are three categories:

  • NEED — Something you must have to survive and function. Without it, your health, safety, or ability to live is at risk. (Food, shelter, clean water, basic clothing)
  • WANT — Something that makes life more enjoyable but you could live without. (Gaming console, designer clothes, concert tickets)
  • GRAY AREA — The interesting ones. Could be a need OR a want depending on your situation, job, health, or where you live. (Smartphone, car, internet, air conditioning)

The magic of this lesson is that there are no perfectly "right" answers for many items. The debate IS the lesson. When a 15-year-old argues that a smartphone is a need and a 9-year-old disagrees, both are learning.

Connection to Lesson 1 (Build Your Life): In the budget challenge, kids had to allocate money to different categories. This lesson zooms in on the very first decision: before you budget, you have to know what you MUST spend on vs. what you CHOOSE to spend on. When they revisit their Build Your Life budgets later, they can audit their own choices with fresh eyes. Ask: "Looking at your budget from last time, did you spend money on any wants that you labeled as needs?"

Lesson Flow

1
Explain the Concept (2 minutes)

Hold up one obvious card from each pile. Say something like:

"Today we're going to figure out the difference between things we NEED and things we WANT. A need is something you absolutely must have to survive and function — like food, a place to live, and clean water. A want is something that makes life fun or more comfortable, but you could survive without it — like a gaming console or fancy sneakers. And some things? They're in between. We call that the Gray Area. Let's see if we agree!"

Show the sorting mat. Point to the three columns. Keep this brief — the game teaches better than the explanation.

2
Sort the Cards Together (10–15 minutes)

Shuffle the item cards. There are two ways to play:

  • Option A (Recommended for first time): You hold up each card and read it aloud. Everyone calls out where they think it goes. If there's disagreement, discuss for 20–30 seconds, then take a vote. Majority wins. Place the card on the mat.
  • Option B (Faster): Deal cards evenly. Each kid sorts their own pile silently, then everyone reviews the placements and challenges any they disagree with.

You do NOT need to get through all 50+ cards. 30–35 is plenty. Pick out the obvious ones and the juicy debate ones. Skip duplicates of the same debate.

Speed tip: Pre-sort the deck. Start with 5–6 easy ones (rent, water, gaming console, designer clothes) so kids get the concept, then move to the gray-area cards that spark real discussion.
3
Situation Flip (5 minutes)

Now the fun part. Pull out the situation cards and read 3–4 aloud. After each one, ask:

"Look at the piles. In THIS situation, does anything need to move?"

Let the kids physically pick up cards and move them. Some examples of what should happen:

  • "You live on a farm 20 miles from town" → Car probably moves from Want/Gray to Need
  • "You live in NYC with great transit" → Car probably moves from Need/Gray to Want
  • "You have a 2-year-old child" → Bigger home, life insurance, maybe a car all shift toward Need
  • "You just lost your job" → Streaming, gym membership, eating out all become clearer Wants

This is the key takeaway: context changes everything. A need for one person is a want for another.

4
Rank Your Wants (5 minutes)

Each kid looks at the Want pile and picks their personal top 5 wants (top 3 for the 6-year-old). They write them on their worksheet and explain WHY each one matters to them.

Then announce: "Uh oh — money is tight. You can only keep 3 of your 5. Cross two off. Which two go?"

This forces real prioritization. Some kids will agonize. That's the point.

5
Group Discussion (5 minutes)

Use these conversation starters (pick 2–3):

  • "Did anyone change their mind about something during the game? What made you change it?"
  • "Was there a card that everyone disagreed about? Why do you think people saw it differently?"
  • "If a family is struggling with money, which wants should they cut first? Which last?"
  • "Can a want BECOME a need? Can you think of an example?"
  • "People 100 years ago didn't have most of these items. Were they missing needs or wants?"
  • "Is internet a need in 2026? What about 10 years ago? What changed?"

Handling Different Ages

Primary (Age 6) K–1 Needs extra help

  • Reading: Read every card aloud. Show the emoji and say the item name.
  • Sorting: Let them physically place cards on the mat. The tactile element helps.
  • Simplify: Start with just NEED and WANT (skip Gray Area). After they get it, introduce "Hmm, this one is tricky..." for gray items.
  • Worksheet: Use the Primary version — pick 3 wants instead of 5, draw pictures instead of writing full sentences.
  • Keep engaged: Let them be the "card flipper" who reveals each card to the group.
  • Simple framing: "Do you HAVE to have this to be safe and healthy? Or is it something fun that you WISH you had?"

Elementary (Ages 8–10) 2nd–4th

  • Can read most cards independently but may need help with cost context ("$150/month — is that a lot?")
  • Help them understand costs by comparing: "$150 is like 15 trips to the dollar store" or "That's about what we spend on groceries each week."
  • They'll often sort in black-and-white (need or want, no gray). Push them: "Is that ALWAYS true? What if..."
  • Standard worksheet is fine for ages 9–10. Use Primary for age 8 if writing is still difficult.

Middle School (Ages 11–13) 5th–7th

  • These kids will argue passionately that their phone is a need. GREAT — make them defend it. "Okay, but WHY is it a need? What specifically would you lose without it?"
  • Push them to think about the difference between "a phone" (maybe a need) and "the newest iPhone" (almost certainly a want).
  • They can handle the situation card exercise independently — let them be the ones deciding what moves.
  • Challenge them: "If you had to cut your family's budget by $500/month, what goes first?"

High School (Ages 14–15) 8th–9th

  • Have them think about this from the perspective of actually living on their own. "You just got your first apartment. Your paycheck after taxes is $2,400/month."
  • Ask them to estimate what percentage of income should go to needs vs. wants. (Common guideline: 50/30/20 — 50% needs, 30% wants, 20% savings)
  • They should write detailed reasoning on the worksheet — not just "because I like it" but real justification.
  • Connect to Build Your Life: "Look at your budget from Lesson 1. How much went to needs? How much to wants? Would you change anything?"

Facilitating Debate Without Arguments

Ground Rules (state these at the start):
  1. "There are no wrong answers for the gray-area cards. If you disagree, explain WHY — don't just say 'no, you're wrong.'"
  2. "Start your sentences with 'I think...' or 'What about when...' not 'That's stupid' or 'No!'"
  3. "If two people disagree, that's actually perfect. It means the card might be a gray-area item."
  4. "Younger kids go first when we vote, so they aren't influenced by older siblings."
When debate gets heated:
  • Redirect to situations: "You're both right! It depends on the situation. Can you think of when it would be a need? When it would be a want?"
  • Use the Gray Area pile as a pressure valve: "Sounds like this one isn't clear-cut. Gray Area it is!"
  • Validate both sides: "Interesting — Sibling A thinks internet is a need because of school. Sibling B thinks it's a want because the library has free WiFi. Both are real arguments."
  • Time-box: If one card takes more than 60 seconds of debate, say "Great discussion! Let's vote and move on."

What Kids Should Walk Away Understanding

  1. Needs and wants aren't always obvious. Many things we think of as needs are actually wants (or at least upgrades of needs).
  2. Context matters. The same item can be a need in one situation and a want in another.
  3. Knowing the difference is the foundation of budgeting. You pay for needs FIRST, then decide which wants fit your budget.
  4. Wants aren't bad. Wants make life worth living. The skill is choosing WHICH wants matter most to you.
  5. Different people value different things. Your sibling's top want might be at the bottom of your list, and that's okay.

If You Have Extra Time

  • Historical comparison: What would this sorting look like in 1926? In 1826? Many "needs" today didn't exist.
  • Budget connection: Pull out Build Your Life budgets. Have each kid identify 2 "want" items in their budget they could cut, and calculate how much they'd save per month / per year.
  • Advertising awareness: "Companies spend billions trying to make you FEEL like wants are needs. Can you think of an ad that does this?" (Great for ages 11+)
  • Create your own card: Each kid designs one item card for something not in the deck.
Common facilitator mistake: Don't correct kids who put "wrong" items in piles during Step 2. The debate corrects itself. If a 6-year-old puts "gaming console" in Needs, don't say "No, that's a want." Ask the group: "What do we think? Could you survive without a gaming console?" Let the siblings teach each other.
