☀️   Florida Financial Facts — The Sunshine State Spotlight   🍊

✅ Advantages

  • No state income tax — save $2,000–$8,000+/year vs. high-tax states
  • No state estate/inheritance tax — your wealth passes to family
  • Homestead exemption — your primary home is protected from most creditors (unique to Florida!)
  • No tax on Social Security or retirement income
  • Lower overall cost of living than CA, NY, NJ, MA, etc.

⚠️ Watch Out For

  • Homeowner's insurance: 2–3× national average due to hurricanes. A $300K home can cost $4,000–$7,000/yr to insure. National avg: ~$1,500.
  • Flood insurance: Required in many areas, NOT included in standard policies. Can add $500–$2,500/yr.
  • Car insurance: FL is a "no-fault" state with many uninsured drivers. Avg: ~$2,800/yr vs. $1,700 nationally.
  • Hurricane preparedness: Budget for storm supplies, potential evacuation costs, deductibles. Hurricane deductible can be 2–5% of home value ($6,000–$15,000 on a $300K home).
  • Property taxes: About 0.86% of home value. On a $300K home: ~$2,580/yr.
  • Sales tax: 6% state + up to 1.5% local = up to 7.5% on purchases.
  • Heat = high electric bills: AC runs most of the year. Average electric: $150–$250/month.

💡 Did You Know?

  • Florida is the #1 destination for people moving from high-tax states like NY and CA.
  • The average Floridian saves $2,000–$5,000/year just from no state income tax.
  • But that savings can be wiped out by one bad hurricane season if you're not properly insured.
  • Florida's homestead exemption is so strong that some people have moved to Florida specifically to protect their home from creditors.
🧭 Section 3: The Lesson — Geography Matters

Discussion Prompts

  • If you took the same job in California vs. Florida, you'd earn the same gross salary but take home different amounts. How much difference does state income tax make?
  • Florida saves you on income tax but costs more for insurance. Which trade-off would you prefer?
  • If you're choosing between two job offers — one in a big expensive city for $80K and one in a small town for $55K — which might actually leave you with MORE money after expenses?
  • Why do you think so many people are moving to Florida and Texas from other states?
  • What would you research about a city's finances BEFORE deciding to move there?
📐 Cost of Living Rule of Thumb:

Before accepting a job in a new city, research: housing costs, state/local taxes, insurance rates, and commute costs.

A $90K salary in San Francisco might leave you with LESS money than a $60K salary in Tampa after accounting for the $2,800/month rent difference.
