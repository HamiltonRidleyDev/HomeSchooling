Build Your Life Budget Challenge

Parent Guide & Facilitator Notes — Version 2

What is this? A hands-on budgeting simulation where each kid picks a career, draws a life situation, builds a monthly budget with real-world prices, faces surprise expenses (where insurance choices matter!), and pitches a side hustle to boost their income. Designed for 6 kids ages 6-15 working simultaneously at different levels.

Time: 30-75 minutes depending on age (can split into 2 sessions)
Materials: Printed worksheets, pencils, calculators (ages 9+), scratch paper, scissors (for cards)

What's New in This Version

  • Career selection — income is earned, not assigned. Kids pick or draw a real career.
  • Life Situation Cards — draw a card that adds real-life constraints (student loans, kids, expensive city, lucky breaks)
  • Real-World Price Catalog — actual prices for groceries, streaming, rent, cars, insurance, and more
  • Advanced concepts — insurance tiers, investing/mutual funds, giving/charity, emergency funds. Explained age-appropriately.
  • Conditional surprise cards — outcomes depend on earlier choices. Skipped renter's insurance? The house fire costs $4,500 instead of $500.
  • Build-your-own budgeting — older kids don't just check boxes; they itemize food, entertainment, and more from the catalog
  • Grade-based naming — Primary, Elementary, Middle School, High School

Recommended: 3-Session Structure

Instead of one long session, break it into three focused sessions. This builds anticipation, prevents fatigue, and lets concepts sink in between sessions.

Session 1 — Career + Life Situation + Basic Budget 40-50 min

1
Set the Scene (3-5 min)
"Congratulations — you're on your own! You have a job, your own place, and a monthly paycheck. But everything costs money — housing, food, your phone, fun stuff, all of it. Can you build a life you love without going broke?"

Optional: Hand out Budget Bucks (play money) as their "paycheck." As they make choices, they physically hand money back. The 6-year-old gets five $100 bills. The 15-year-old gets their career income in bills.
2
Pick a Career (5 min)
Lay out the Career Cards. Let each kid browse and pick one that interests them — OR cut the cards apart and draw randomly for extra fun. They write their career and income on their worksheet. Younger kids pick from the simplified options on their sheet.
3
Draw a Life Situation (3 min)
Shuffle the Life Situation Cards face-down. Each kid draws one (age 15 draws two). Read it aloud, discuss briefly, then they apply the impact to their budget. This creates the "everyone has a different situation" dynamic.
4
Build the Basic Budget (20-30 min)
Kids work through the core budget categories (housing, utilities, food, transportation, phone, entertainment, clothing). The Price Catalog stays in the center of the table. Help the youngest, let the middle kids work independently, challenge the oldest.

Stop here for Session 1. Let them add up their basic categories and see where they stand. End with: "Next time, we'll talk about some things adults know about that can protect you or grow your money..."

Session 2 — Smart Money + Full Tracker + Pie Chart 35-45 min

5
Quick Recap (3 min)
"Last time you picked a career, drew your life situation, and started your budget. Today we're going to talk about the things that separate people who are good with money from people who aren't."
6
Smart Money Choices (15-20 min)
Walk through each advanced concept before they choose. Pause and explain insurance, investing, savings, emergency fund, and giving. Use the explanations in the "Teaching Advanced Concepts" section below. Let each kid make their choices and add them to the budget.
7
The Reality Check + Pie Chart (10-15 min)
Everyone completes their full budget tracker. Total it up. Over or under? Then hand out the Pie Chart Template — they calculate percentages and color in their budget breakdown. Compare to the 50/30/20 rule. Ask: "What would you cut? What surprised you?"
8
Pick Your Investments (10 min, ages 9+)
Hand out the Investment Tracker. Kids who chose to invest pick 2-3 real stocks or index funds. Look up current prices together (Google "[ticker] stock price"). They record starting prices and calculate shares. This is what you'll track at quarterly check-ins!

Session 3 — Surprise Cards + Side Hustle Shark Tank 30-40 min

9
Surprise Cards! (10 min, ages 9+)
Each kid draws 1 Expense card and 1 Windfall card. This is where insurance choices pay off (or don't). The kid who paid $20/mo for renter's insurance only pays $500 for the house fire — the kid who skipped it pays $4,500. Read the lesson on each card aloud. Discuss.
10
Side Hustle "Shark Tank" (15-20 min)
"Want more from life? Don't just wish for it — earn it." Each kid fills out their entrepreneurial pitch (5-8 min) and then presents it to the family. Family asks questions. Vote on the best pitch. If it's realistic, they add the income to their budget and get to upgrade one choice.
11
Share, Compare & Celebrate (10 min)
Everyone presents their complete "life." Compare choices across the table. There are no wrong answers — just different priorities and consequences. End with ice cream or a treat and the question: "What's one thing you learned about money that you didn't know before?"
Pro Tip: Keep it fun, not stressful. If the youngest gets frustrated with math, help them. If the oldest is overthinking, remind them there's no perfect answer. The goal is the THINKING process, not a perfect budget.
Key principle: If a child is way over budget, that's the teachable moment — celebrate the struggle, don't fix it for them. The frustration of "I can't afford everything I want" IS the lesson. Let them sit with it, then ask: "What would you cut?"
Alternative: If 3 sessions doesn't work for your schedule, you can absolutely run it as one longer session (75-90 min with a break). The session breaks above are natural stopping points.

Teaching the Advanced Concepts

These are the "things kids wouldn't know to think about" — here's how to introduce each one:

Insurance

Simple explanation: "You pay a small amount every month. If something terrible happens — fire, car crash, hospital — the insurance company pays most of it. Without it, YOU pay everything."

The moment it clicks: When they draw a Surprise Card and see the difference. The kid with renter's insurance pays $500 for a fire. The kid without pays $4,500. That's a year's worth of insurance premiums saved in one event.

Investing / Mutual Funds

Simple explanation: "A mutual fund or index fund is like a basket — instead of buying one company's stock, you buy a tiny piece of 500 companies. If some go down, others go up. Over decades, it tends to grow about 10% per year."

The wow number: "$100/month invested from age 20 becomes $632,000 by age 60. You only put in $48,000. The rest is growth." (This is on the Price Catalog.)

Giving / Charity

Simple explanation: "Some people give 10% of their income (a tithe). Some give to causes they care about. Some volunteer time instead. It's a personal choice, but generous people often find it comes back to them in unexpected ways."

The card connection: The "Community Payback" windfall card rewards kids who chose to give.

Emergency Fund

Simple explanation: "This is money you don't touch unless something goes wrong — car breaks, lose your job, medical bill. Experts recommend 3-6 months of expenses saved up."

The moment it clicks: When they draw a Surprise Card and their emergency fund covers it vs. having to go into debt.

Credit Cards (Age 15)

Simple explanation: "A credit card lets you buy now and pay later. BUT if you don't pay it all off each month, they charge you interest — usually 20-25%. A $1,000 TV can end up costing $1,580 if you only make minimum payments."

Key teaching: Credit cards aren't evil — they build credit and offer protections. The danger is carrying a balance.

Conversation Starters

During Budget Building

  • "Why did you pick that over the cheaper option? Is it worth the difference?"
  • "If you had to cut $200 right now, what goes first?"
  • "Look at how much of your money goes to housing. Does that feel like a lot?"
  • "Did anything on the Price Catalog surprise you?"

About Insurance & Protection

  • "What's the worst thing that could happen if you don't have renter's insurance?"
  • "Is it worth paying $20/month to protect yourself from a $4,500 loss?"
  • "What if you never need the insurance — was it wasted money?"

About Investing & Savings

  • "If you invest $100/month starting now, how much would you have at age 60?"
  • "What's the difference between saving and investing?"
  • "Why is starting young such a big advantage?"

About the Side Hustle

  • "What's a problem you see every day that you could solve?"
  • "How is your side hustle different from just getting a second job?"
  • "What would you do with the extra income — spend it or invest it?"

Big Picture

  • "Would you rather have a nicer home or a nicer car? Why?"
  • "Is it possible to have everything you want? What has to give?"
  • "What would happen if you lost your job for 3 months? Could you survive?"
  • "What's one thing you'd tell your future self about money?"

Extending This Lesson

  • Week 2 — Price Check Field Trip: Go to a grocery store. Each kid compares catalog prices to real shelf prices. Update their food budget with real numbers.
  • Week 3 — Savings & Compound Interest Deep Dive: Use the investing numbers from the catalog. Calculate growth at different amounts. "What if you started at 15 vs. 25 vs. 35?"
  • Week 4 — Try the Side Hustle: Pick one kid's pitch and actually try it for a week. Track real income and expenses.
  • Week 5 — Revisit & Adjust: Bring the budgets back out. "Would you change anything now that you've thought about it?" Real budgets get adjusted every month.
  • Ongoing — Real Allowance Budget: Give each kid a monthly allowance and have them manage it using what they learned.

Quarterly Check-Ins (Every 3 Months)

This is where the lesson becomes a lifelong habit instead of a one-time exercise.

  • Print fresh Quarterly Check-In sheets for each kid (15 min)
  • Look up current stock prices together — update the Investment Tracker
  • Discuss: What happened in the market? Did your picks go up or down? Why?
  • Review side hustle progress — did anyone actually try theirs?
  • Update savings goals
  • Set goals for next quarter

After a full year, the Investment Tracker year-end review is a powerful moment — they can see real growth (or loss) and understand that investing is a long game.
