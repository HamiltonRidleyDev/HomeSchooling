Scam Examples Gallery

Learn to spot these common scams. Every example below is fake — created to teach you what real scams look like. Keep this as a reference.

1
Phishing Email

What it looks like: An email that appears to be from your bank, warning you about a problem with your account.

From: security-alert@bnk0famerica-verify.com
Subject: URGENT: Your Account Has Been Suspended
To: Dear Valued Customer

We have detected suspicious activity on your account. Your account has been temporarily suspended for your protection.

You must verify your identity within 24 hours or your account will be permanently closed.

Click here to verify your account: http://bnk0famerica-verify.com/login

Bank of America Security Team

RED FLAGS:

  1. Suspicious sender address — "bnk0famerica-verify.com" is NOT a real Bank of America address (note the zero instead of "o")
  2. Generic greeting — "Dear Valued Customer" instead of your actual name
  3. Urgency and threats — "24 hours or permanently closed" pressures you to act fast
  4. Suspicious link — The URL doesn't go to bankofamerica.com

What to do instead:

Never click links in emails about your bank. Open a new browser window and go directly to your bank's website by typing the address yourself (bankofamerica.com), or call the number on the back of your bank card.

2
Fake Text Message (Smishing)

What it looks like: A text that seems to be from a delivery company about a package.

+1 (347) 555-0199
USPS: Your package cannot be delivered. Delivery address is incomplete. Update your address to schedule redelivery: usps-redelivery-update.com/track?id=83927

RED FLAGS:

  1. Random phone number — USPS doesn't text from random numbers
  2. Suspicious link — "usps-redelivery-update.com" is not the real USPS website (usps.com)
  3. Vague details — No tracking number, no specific package information
  4. Unsolicited — You may not even be expecting a package

What to do instead:

Don't click the link. If you're expecting a package, go directly to usps.com and enter your tracking number there. Delete the text.

3
Social Media Scam

What it looks like: A pop-up or ad on social media offering free prizes.

Congratulations!!! You've been randomly selected!

You've won a FREE iPhone 16 Pro Max (worth $1,199)!

Just pay $4.99 shipping to claim your prize!

Enter your credit card information below to cover shipping costs.

Offer expires in 3:47... 3:46... 3:45...

RED FLAGS:

  1. Too good to be true — Nobody gives away $1,199 phones to random people
  2. Asks for credit card — "Free" things that require payment information are not free
  3. Countdown timer — Fake urgency to prevent you from thinking it over
  4. Excessive exclamation marks — Professional companies don't write like this

What to do instead:

Close it immediately. You can't win a contest you never entered. Never enter credit card information on pop-ups or ads. Report the ad if possible.

4
Phone Call Scam (IRS Impersonation)

What it looks like: A voicemail or live call from someone claiming to be the IRS or police.

[Robotic voice] "This is Officer James Wilson with the Internal Revenue Service. Our records show you owe $3,500 in unpaid back taxes. A warrant has been issued for your arrest. To resolve this matter and avoid arrest, you must make an immediate payment using prepaid gift cards. Press 1 to speak with an agent now. This is your final notice."

RED FLAGS:

  1. Threatening arrest — The IRS does not threaten arrest over the phone
  2. Demanding gift cards — No government agency accepts gift cards as payment. Ever.
  3. Robotic voice — Real IRS agents are real people, and they contact you by mail first
  4. "Final notice" pressure — Designed to make you panic and act immediately
  5. No specific details — Doesn't use your name or give a real case number

What to do instead:

Hang up. The real IRS always contacts you by mail first and never demands immediate payment by gift cards, wire transfers, or cryptocurrency. If concerned, call the IRS directly at their official number: 1-800-829-1040.

5
Fake Website (Look-Alike Login Page)

What it looks like: A login page that looks exactly like a real website, but the URL is slightly different.

FAKE
amaz0n-verify.com/signin

Sign In

Email:  

Password:  

Looks identical to the real page!

REAL
🔒 amazon.com/signin

Sign In

Email:  

Password:  

Check the URL carefully!

RED FLAGS:

  1. URL is wrong — "amaz0n-verify.com" uses a zero instead of the letter "o" and adds extra words
  2. No padlock icon — Real sites show a lock symbol in the address bar
  3. How you got there — You probably arrived via a link in a suspicious email instead of typing the address yourself

What to do instead:

Always check the URL before entering any login information. Type website addresses yourself instead of clicking links. Look for the padlock icon. When in doubt, open a new tab and navigate to the site directly.

6
Romance / Friend Scam

What it looks like: A message from someone you know (or think you know) asking for money urgently.

From: Uncle Dave (Facebook Messenger)

"Hey! I'm so embarrassed to ask but I'm stuck in Mexico — I was robbed and lost my wallet and passport. The embassy says I need $500 to get emergency travel documents. Can you wire me money through Western Union? I'll pay you back as soon as I'm home. Please don't tell anyone, I'm so embarrassed. Here's the info to send to..."

RED FLAGS:

  1. Urgent money request — Asks for money wired quickly, which is hard to trace or reverse
  2. "Don't tell anyone" — Scammers isolate you so no one can warn you
  3. Account may be hacked — The real person's account was likely taken over by a scammer
  4. Emotional pressure — Uses your relationship and sympathy against you

What to do instead:

Contact "Uncle Dave" through a different method — call his phone number, text him, or ask another family member. Never send money based solely on a social media message. If the account was hacked, the real person needs to know.

7
Too-Good-To-Be-True Job Scam

What it looks like: An ad or email promising huge money for easy work.

WORK FROM HOME - EARN $5,000/WEEK!

No experience needed! No interview required!
Just 2 hours a day from your phone!
We need 50 people in your area RIGHT NOW.
Send your full name, address, date of birth, and bank account number to get started.

RED FLAGS:

  1. Unrealistic pay — $5,000/week for 2 hours a day is $1,250/hour. No legitimate job pays this for unskilled work.
  2. No experience or interview — Real employers want to know who they're hiring
  3. Asks for bank information upfront — A real employer only needs banking info after you've been hired, for direct deposit
  4. Artificial scarcity — "50 people RIGHT NOW" pressures you to apply before thinking

What to do instead:

If it sounds too good to be true, it is. Legitimate jobs have real company names, real interviews, and realistic pay. Never share bank account details, your Social Security number, or other personal information in response to an unsolicited job offer.

8
QR Code Scam

What it looks like: A fake QR code sticker placed over a real one in a public place — like a parking meter, restaurant table, or event poster.

[A parking meter with a QR code sticker]

SCAN TO PAY FOR PARKING

But the sticker was placed by a scammer over the real QR code. When you scan it, you're taken to a fake payment site that steals your credit card information.

RED FLAGS:

  1. Sticker over sticker — Look for signs that a QR code has been stuck on top of another one (edges peeling, different paper)
  2. Check the URL after scanning — Before entering any information, look at what website the QR code actually sends you to
  3. Public locations — QR codes in places where anyone could tamper with them are higher risk

What to do instead:

After scanning any QR code, check the URL it takes you to before entering information. If it looks suspicious, close it. For parking meters, use the official app or pay at the machine directly. When in doubt, type the website address manually instead of scanning.

9
Virus / Tech Support Scam

What it looks like: A scary pop-up that appears while browsing the web.

WARNING! YOUR COMPUTER IS INFECTED!

Windows has detected (5) critical viruses on your system.
Your personal data, banking information, and passwords are at risk.

Call Microsoft Support IMMEDIATELY:
1-888-555-0147

DO NOT CLOSE THIS WINDOW OR RESTART YOUR COMPUTER

RED FLAGS:

  1. Pop-up "virus" warnings — Real antivirus software doesn't show pop-ups in your browser
  2. Phone number to call — Microsoft will never ask you to call them through a browser pop-up
  3. Panic language — "IMMEDIATELY" and "DO NOT close" are designed to scare you into obeying
  4. Fake specifics — "(5) critical viruses" sounds precise but is completely made up

What to do instead:

Close the browser tab (or force-close the browser if needed: Ctrl+Alt+Delete on Windows, or Command+Q on Mac). Do NOT call the number. If you're worried about real viruses, run your actual antivirus software — the one you installed, not something a website told you about.

10
"Hi Mom" Text Scam

What it looks like: A text message pretending to be from your child or family member with a "new phone number."

+1 (512) 555-0834
Hi Mom! It's Sarah. I got a new phone and this is my new number. My old phone broke so I can't use that number anymore. I'm in a bit of a jam — can you send me $200 through Venmo or Zelle? I'll explain everything later. Love you!

RED FLAGS:

  1. New unknown number — Conveniently explains why it's not from the contact you know
  2. Money request — Quick pivot to asking for money via apps that are hard to reverse
  3. Vague emergency — "I'll explain later" avoids giving details you could verify
  4. Could apply to anyone — Scammers send thousands of these hoping some recipients have a "Sarah"

What to do instead:

Call your actual child at their known phone number. If their phone is really broken, call them at a friend's number, their school, or contact them through another family member. Never send money based on a text from an unknown number, no matter who they claim to be.

Keep This Sheet!
The #1 rule of digital safety: If a message creates URGENCY or FEAR, that itself is the biggest red flag.
STOP. THINK. VERIFY through an official channel you find yourself.
