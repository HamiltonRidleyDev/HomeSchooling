For Our Youngest Learners Ages 6-7

Three Rules to Remember:

  1. Never share your password with anyone except Mom or Dad. Not friends, not teachers, not anyone online.
  2. Never type personal information (your name, address, school, or birthday) on a website without asking a parent first.
  3. If something feels weird or scary on a screen, don't click anything — go tell a parent right away.

The Secret: Length beats complexity. A long passphrase made of random words is both stronger AND easier to remember than a short password full of symbols. Four random words separated by dashes = extremely hard to crack.

Round 2: Build a Passphrase

Pick 4 random, unrelated words and put them together. The weirder the combination, the better (and easier to remember)!

Tips:

  • Use words that create a funny picture in your mind
  • Don't use words that go together naturally (like "peanut-butter-jelly-time")
  • Add dashes or spaces between words
  • Avoid personal info (pet names, birthdays, addresses)

What Is a Password Manager?

A password manager is an app that acts like a super-secure digital vault. It:

  • Remembers all your passwords so you don't have to
  • Creates super-strong random passwords for every account
  • Fills in your passwords automatically when you log in
  • Requires one master password to unlock — that's the only one you memorize

Why use one? The average person has 80-100 online accounts. Nobody can remember 100 different strong passwords. A password manager means every account gets a unique, strong password, and you only remember one.

Popular password managers: 1Password, Bitwarden (free), Apple Keychain (built into iPhones/Macs), Google Password Manager (built into Chrome)

The #1 password mistake: Using the same password for multiple accounts. If one account gets hacked, the hackers try that same password on every other site — your email, your bank, everything. A password manager solves this completely.

What Is Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)?

Two-factor authentication adds a second lock to your account. Even if someone steals your password, they still can't get in without the second factor.

How it works:

Think of it like this: Your password is like a key to your house. 2FA is like also needing your fingerprint to open the door. A thief might copy your key, but they can't copy your fingerprint.

Where to turn on 2FA first (most important accounts):

  1. Email — If someone gets into your email, they can reset every other password
  2. Bank accounts — Protects your money directly
  3. Social media — Prevents someone from impersonating you

Family Security Checklist

Go through this together as a family. Check off what you've done, and make a plan for what's left.

  • No one reuses the same password for banking and other sites
  • All important accounts have 2FA turned on (email, banking, social media)
  • Everyone knows: never click links in unexpected texts or emails — go to the website directly
  • Everyone knows: if a message seems urgent, STOP and verify through official channels (call the real number, visit the real website)
  • Parent's phone number is memorized by every child (not just saved in phone contacts)
  • Family has a password manager set up (or has a plan to set one up this week)
  • Devices have a screen lock (PIN, fingerprint, or face recognition)
  • Everyone knows: no government agency ever asks for gift card payments
  • We review bank and credit card statements regularly for unrecognized charges
  • If anyone in the family encounters a scam attempt, they share it with the group so everyone can learn
Family project this week: Pick one item from the checklist that isn't done yet and complete it together. Small steps build strong habits.
