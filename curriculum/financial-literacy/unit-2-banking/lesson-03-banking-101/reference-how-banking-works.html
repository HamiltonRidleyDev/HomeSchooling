What is a Debit Card?

A debit card is like a magic key to your bank account. When you use it at a store, it takes money out of your account and gives it to the store. It only works if you have money in your account!

Remember: A debit card spends YOUR money. If you have $10 and spend $10, you have $0 left.

Three Things to Remember

  1. The bank keeps your money safe
  2. The bank gives you extra money (interest) for keeping it there
  3. A debit card uses your money from the bank

Two Kinds of Bank Accounts

💳

Checking Account

Your everyday account. Money goes in and out all the time. Comes with a debit card. Used to pay for things.

🏦

Savings Account

Your "don't touch" account. Money sits here and earns interest. Save up for big goals here.

What is Interest?

Think of it this way

The bank pays you rent for using your money. You let the bank borrow your money, and they pay you a small amount in return. The more money you keep there, and the longer you leave it, the more interest you earn.

Example: If you have $100 in savings and the bank pays 4% interest, after one year you'd have $104. You earned $4 just for leaving your money there!

Debit Card vs Cash

  • Cash: Physical bills and coins. Once you spend it, it's gone. You can see exactly how much you have.
  • Debit card: Spends money from your checking account electronically. Faster and more convenient, but harder to track how much you're spending.
  • They both use YOUR money — just in different forms.

What is an Overdraft?

Warning: This Costs You Money!

An overdraft happens when you try to spend more money than you have in your account. The bank might let the payment go through, but they charge you a fee — usually around $35!

Example: You have $8 in your account. You buy something for $10. The bank pays the store $10 but charges you a $35 overdraft fee. Now you owe the bank $37. That $10 item actually cost you $45!

How Banks Make Money

The Business of Banking

Banks are businesses. Here's how they make money:

  1. You deposit $1,000 into your savings account. The bank pays you 4% interest ($40/year).
  2. The bank lends your $1,000 to someone else — say, for a car loan — and charges them 8% interest ($80/year).
  3. The bank keeps the difference: $80 − $40 = $40 profit.

This is called the interest rate spread. Multiply this by millions of customers and you can see why banks are profitable businesses.

FDIC Insurance

Your Money is Guaranteed

The Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) is a government agency that protects your money. If your bank goes out of business, the FDIC guarantees your deposits up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank.

This means a bank account is one of the safest places to keep money. Your cash under a mattress has zero protection.

Direct Deposit

Instead of getting a paper check from your job and bringing it to the bank, your employer sends your paycheck electronically straight into your checking account. It's faster, safer, and automatic. Most employers use direct deposit.

APY — Annual Percentage Yield

The Real Way to Compare Banks

APY is the total amount of interest you earn in one year, including compound interest (interest on your interest). It's the number you should compare when choosing where to put your savings.

Example: Bank A offers 4.25% APY. Bank B offers 4.50% APY. On $5,000:

  • Bank A: $5,000 × 0.0425 = $212.50/year
  • Bank B: $5,000 × 0.0450 = $225.00/year
  • Difference: $12.50/year — not huge, but it adds up over time and with larger balances.

Online Banks vs Traditional Banks

Online banks (like Ally, Marcus, Discover) have no physical branches. Because they don't pay for buildings and tellers, they can offer:

  • Higher interest rates on savings (often 4–5x more than traditional banks)
  • Lower or no monthly fees
  • Free ATM access through networks

The trade-off: No branch to walk into. Everything is done through an app or website. For most people under 30, this is fine. For someone who wants to deposit cash frequently, a traditional bank may be better.

How to Read a Bank Statement

What to Look For Every Month
  1. Starting balance — Should match last month's ending balance
  2. Deposits — Did your paycheck(s) arrive? All of them?
  3. Withdrawals — Do you recognize every single charge? If not, investigate.
  4. Fees — Any overdraft fees, monthly maintenance fees, or ATM fees?
  5. Ending balance — Does the math add up? Starting + deposits − withdrawals = ending.
  6. Suspicious charges — Small charges ($1–$5) from companies you don't recognize could be fraud. Report them.

What is a Credit Score and Why Banks Care

Your Financial Reputation

A credit score is a number (300–850) that represents how trustworthy you are with borrowed money. Banks, landlords, and even some employers check it.

What affects it:

  • Payment history (35%) — Do you pay bills on time?
  • Credit utilization (30%) — How much of your available credit are you using?
  • Length of credit history (15%) — How long have you had credit accounts?
  • Credit mix (10%) — Do you have different types of credit?
  • New credit (10%) — Have you applied for lots of credit recently?

Why it matters now: You don't have a credit score yet, but you will soon. When you turn 18, opening a credit card and paying it off every month is one of the best ways to start building credit. We'll cover this more in a future lesson.

The goal: checking account for daily spending, high-yield savings for your emergency fund, and eventually a credit card (paid in full every month) to build credit.
