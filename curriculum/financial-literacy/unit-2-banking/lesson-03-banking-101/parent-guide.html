Unit 2: Banking — Lesson 3

Banking 101: Your Money's Home

Parent / Facilitator Guide
Duration: 30–40 minutes
Ages: 6–15 (6 kids)
Format: Guided discussion + hands-on analysis
Prep time: 5 minutes (print materials)

Learning Objectives

  • Understand what a bank does and why people use one
  • Know the difference between checking, savings, and high-yield savings accounts
  • Read and analyze a real bank statement
  • Distinguish between debit cards and credit cards
  • Spot fees, suspicious charges, and overdrafts

Materials Needed

reference-how-banking-works.html — print one per kid (they each read their age tier)
mock-bank-statement.html — print one per kid or pair
worksheet-bank-detective.html — print one per kid
Pencils, calculator (optional), highlighters for marking up the statement
Connection to Build Your Life
In the budgeting unit, the kids built a budget for their fictional life. Ask them: "You planned where every dollar would go — but where does that money actually sit? Where does your paycheck land? Where do your bill payments come from?" That is what today's lesson answers. The budget is the plan; the bank account is where the plan lives.

Lesson Flow

1Hook — Where Does Money Live?
3 min
Say This
"Where does money live? Not in your wallet — that's just visiting. Your money lives at the bank. Think of it like this: your wallet is a hotel room. The bank is your money's actual house."

Ask the kids: Why wouldn't you just keep all your money in a shoebox under your bed?

Guide them toward three reasons:

  1. Safety — it could get stolen, lost in a fire, or eaten by the dog
  2. Growth — the bank actually pays you to keep money there (interest)
  3. Access — you can spend it with a card, pay bills online, send money to people

Briefly explain what the bank does with your money: "They don't just put it in a vault with your name on it. They lend it to other people — to buy houses, start businesses — and those people pay the bank interest. The bank shares a little of that interest with you as a thank-you for letting them use your money."

2Account Types Lesson
10 min

Hand out the reference-how-banking-works sheet. Each kid reads their age-appropriate tier. Walk through the three main account types together:

Checking Account

  • Your everyday spending account
  • Money goes in (paycheck, deposits) and out (bills, purchases)
  • Comes with a debit card
  • Earns almost zero interest (typically 0.01%)
  • Think of it as your money's "front door" — always in motion

Savings Account

  • Your money's storage room — for money you don't need right now
  • Earns more interest than checking (around 0.5%–1% at most traditional banks)
  • May have limits on how often you can withdraw
  • No debit card — you transfer money to checking when you need it

High-Yield Savings Account

  • Same as savings, but at online banks that pay much more interest (4%–5% currently)
  • The trade-off: no physical branch to walk into
  • This is where an emergency fund should live
Key Concept: FDIC Insurance
Your money in the bank is guaranteed by the federal government up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank. If the bank goes out of business, the government gives you your money back. This is why a bank account is safer than a shoebox.
Age 6 — Simplified
Focus only on: the bank keeps your money safe, and it gives you a little extra money for keeping it there. Use the piggy bank analogy from the reference sheet. Skip high-yield savings and FDIC details.
Ages 9–10
They should understand checking vs savings and why you'd use each. Introduce the concept of overdraft: "If you try to spend more than you have, the bank charges you a fee."
Ages 11–13
Full account types discussion. They should understand how banks make money (borrow low, lend high). Introduce FDIC, direct deposit, and high-yield savings.
Age 15
Full discussion plus APY, bank comparison, credit unions vs banks, and online banking. Ask: "If you were opening a bank account today, what would you look for?"
3Mock Bank Statement Exercise
15 min

Hand out the mock-bank-statement and the worksheet-bank-detective.

This is the core of the lesson. The kids will analyze a realistic bank statement for "Alex Johnson" and answer questions at their age level.

How to Run It

  1. Give everyone 2 minutes to just look at the statement. Ask: "What do you notice?"
  2. Walk through the first few transactions together so everyone understands the format: date, description, amount, running balance.
  3. Let them work on the worksheet. Younger kids can work in pairs with an older sibling.
  4. After 10 minutes, discuss answers together.
Teaching Moments to Highlight
  • The overdraft fee ($35) — Alex spent more than was in the account, even briefly, and the bank charged $35 for it. "That coffee just cost $35 extra."
  • The $1.00 mystery charge — This is a verification hold from an online merchant. It's common and usually refunded, but it's a lesson in always checking your statement. If you don't recognize a charge, call the bank.
  • Coffee habit — Three coffee purchases in one month. Small amounts that add up. What's the yearly cost?
  • Savings transfer — The one responsible thing Alex does consistently: moving $200 to savings every month.
Age 6 — Simplified
Partner with an older sibling. They find and circle: the biggest number going in (paycheck), the biggest number going out (rent), and the coffee purchases. Count the coffee cups. They answer only the "For Everyone" questions with help.
Ages 9–10
Complete "For Everyone" and "Ages 9+" sections. They should be able to find the overdraft and calculate the coffee total. Help with the interest calculation if needed.
Ages 11–13
Complete all sections through "Ages 13+." They should calculate income, expenses, net, and spending percentages. Introduce the 50/30/20 rule if they haven't seen it.
Age 15
Complete the full worksheet including designing a better budget. They should be able to work completely independently. Challenge: present their improved budget to the group.
4Debit vs Credit Deep Dive
5 min
Say This
"A debit card and a credit card look almost identical. They're the same size, same shape, both have numbers on the front. But they work completely differently."

Draw this on a whiteboard or just explain clearly:

  • Debit card = spends YOUR money, right now, from your checking account. When you swipe, the money leaves your account immediately. If you have $50, you can spend $50. That's it.
  • Credit card = spends the BANK'S money. You're borrowing. At the end of the month, you get a bill. If you don't pay it all back, they charge you interest — and credit card interest is very high (20%+).

Ask the kids: "Which one is safer to use if you're bad at tracking your spending? Why?"

(Answer: debit, because you can only spend what you have. But credit cards have better fraud protection — save that nuance for the 15-year-old.)

For the 15-year-old
Mention that responsible credit card use builds your credit score, which affects your ability to rent an apartment, get a car loan, or buy a house later. "It's a tool — powerful if used right, dangerous if used wrong." This is a preview of a future lesson on credit.
5Discussion + Wrap-Up
5 min

Bring it together with a few discussion questions:

  • "What surprised you about Alex's bank statement?"
  • "If you had a bank account, would you pick checking or savings first? Why?"
  • "What's one thing you'd do differently than Alex?"
Connect Back to Build Your Life
"Remember your budget from the budgeting unit? Every dollar you planned — your rent, groceries, savings — all of that flows through a bank account. The budget is the plan. The bank account is where the plan actually happens. Next time you look at your Build Your Life budget, think about which transactions would show up on your bank statement."

Optional Real-World Field Trip: Visit a Bank

Consider taking the kids to a local bank or credit union. Many banks will do a short tour for homeschool groups and explain how accounts work.

  • Call ahead and ask if they do educational visits for kids
  • Ask if any of the kids (especially the 15-year-old) can open a real savings account with a small deposit ($25–$50)
  • Have each kid prepare one question to ask the banker
  • Look for a bank or credit union with no monthly fees on youth accounts
  • After the visit, have each kid write one thing they learned that surprised them

Prep tip: Credit unions are often more welcoming for these kinds of visits and typically have youth savings accounts with no fees and no minimum balance.

Quick Answer Key (Statement Exercise)

Full answers for facilitator reference. Do not distribute to students.

For Everyone

  • Starting balance: $2,450.00
  • Total deposits/income: $2,100.00 + $2,100.00 = $4,200.00
  • Biggest expense: Rent — $1,100.00
  • Coffee purchases: 3 times, totaling $5.75 + $6.25 + $5.50 = $17.50

For Ages 9+

  • Overdraft fee: $35.00 — it happened because Alex's balance dipped below $0 after a transaction
  • Coffee per year: $17.50/month × 12 = $210.00/year
  • Interest on $200 at 4.5%: $200 × 0.045 = $9.00 per year
  • Suspicious $1.00 charge: Alex should review it, and if unrecognized, call the bank to dispute it. This is likely a verification hold from an online purchase, which should be refunded.

For Ages 13+

  • Total income: $4,200.00
  • Total expenses: $1,100 + $87.50 + $62.30 + $94.15 + $45.00 + $38.75 + $15.49 + $5.75 + $6.25 + $5.50 + $60.00 + $200.00 + $35.00 + $1.00 = $1,756.69
  • Net: $4,200.00 − $1,756.69 = $2,443.31
  • Housing % of income: $1,100 / $4,200 = 26.2%
  • Food % of income: ($87.50 + $62.30 + $94.15) / $4,200 = 5.8%
  • Savings % of income: $200 / $4,200 = 4.8%
  • Wants %: ($15.49 + $17.50 + $45.00 + $38.75 + $60.00) / $4,200 = 4.2% (Netflix, coffee, gas if categorized as transport, ATM)
  • 50/30/20 check: Needs are around 32%, wants around 4.2%, savings is only 4.8%. Alex has a lot of unallocated money ($2,443.31 net) and should be saving significantly more.
  • To save $500/month: Alex already has the income. They just need to set up a $500 transfer instead of $200. The spending is well below income — the issue is that unallocated money likely gets spent on things not showing in this single month.
