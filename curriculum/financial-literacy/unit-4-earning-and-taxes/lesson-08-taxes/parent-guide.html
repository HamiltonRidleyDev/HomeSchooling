Lesson Overview

This lesson answers the question every kid eventually asks: "Why does the government take money from people?" Instead of a lecture, we flip it. In Part A, every kid becomes the government and must decide how to spend $100 in taxes. In Part B, the older kids see exactly where the money goes when they look at a real pay stub.

Before You Begin

Navigating Political Questions

This lesson is about how taxes work, not about political opinions. Kids (especially the older ones) may express strong views they've absorbed. Here's how to stay neutral:

  • Redirect to mechanics: "That's an interesting opinion! In this lesson, we're focused on understanding HOW taxes work, so when you're older you can form your own views."
  • Acknowledge all sides: "Some people think taxes should be higher so the government can do more. Others think they should be lower so people keep more of their money. Both sides have reasons."
  • Use the exercise: "That's exactly what the budget exercise is about — there's no right answer, just trade-offs."
  • Avoid labeling: Don't use terms like "liberal," "conservative," or name specific politicians. Keep it about the system.
Age Notes
  • Ages 6–8: Use the simplified version of the Tax Budget Exercise (6 categories, 10 tokens). Focus on the concept: "Everyone chips in to pay for things we all share." Don't worry about percentages.
  • Ages 9–12: Use the full Tax Budget Exercise. They can handle the real categories and adding to $100. Help with math if needed.
  • Ages 13–15: Full Tax Budget Exercise PLUS Part B (pay stub). These kids are close to getting real jobs — this is directly relevant to their near future.
Part A

"You're the Government" — All Ages, 15–20 Minutes

1

Hook — The Big Question (2 minutes)

"Every time someone earns money, the government takes a piece. Why? Where does it go? And here's the fun part — what would YOU do with it?"

Let them react. They'll probably say things like "That's not fair!" or "Why do they take it?" That's perfect — lean into the curiosity.

"What if I told you that taxes pay for roads, for schools, for the military, for helping people who are sick — basically for almost everything the government does? The question isn't really WHETHER to collect taxes. It's HOW MUCH and WHERE it goes. And today, YOU get to decide."
2

The Visual Breakdown (5 minutes)

Hold up or display the Reference: Where Taxes Go sheet. Walk through each category, starting from the biggest.

"Look at this. The BIGGEST chunk — 25 cents out of every dollar — goes to healthcare. Medicare and Medicaid. That's paying for grandma's doctor visits, or helping people who can't afford to go to the hospital."

Move through the categories. For each one, pause and ask:

"Did you know taxes pay for THAT?"

Hit the ones that surprise kids most:

  • NASA and science — "Your tax dollars fund space exploration!"
  • National parks — "Every time we visit a national park, taxes helped keep it open."
  • Interest on debt — "The government borrows money too, just like people. And 10 cents of every dollar goes just to paying interest. Not even paying it OFF — just the interest."
Ages 6–8

Simplify: "Taxes are when everyone chips in money to pay for things we all share — like roads, schools, firefighters, and parks. It's like everyone putting money in a big jar so we can buy things for the whole neighborhood."

3

The Budget Exercise (8–10 minutes)

Hand out the Tax Budget Exercise worksheet.

"OK, here's the deal. You just got elected President — or King or Queen, whatever you want. The American people paid $100 in taxes. You MUST spend all $100. You can't keep any. You can't throw any away. You have to decide: how much goes to each category?"

Key rules to emphasize:

  • The total MUST equal $100. No more, no less.
  • You can put $0 in a category, but you have to accept what that means (no military? no schools?).
  • There's no right answer. This is about trade-offs.
Facilitator Tip

Walk around as they work. If a kid puts $50 in military and $0 in education, don't correct them — just ask: "Interesting! So no money for schools. What happens to schools then?" Let them think through consequences.

Ages 6–8: Simplified Version

Use the bottom section of the worksheet. Give each young child 10 tokens (coins, stickers, or small objects). They place tokens in 6 picture categories: schools, doctors, soldiers, roads, parks, and helping people. Same concept, simpler execution.

4

Share and Compare (5 minutes)

Go around the table. Each kid shares their allocation.

"OK, let's hear it. Where did you put your money?"

After everyone shares, drive the comparison:

  • "Look how different your budgets are! That's exactly what happens in real life — people disagree about where tax money should go."
  • "Did anyone put $0 somewhere? What would happen if we really did that?"
  • "What was the hardest trade-off? What did you WANT to fund more but couldn't?"
"This is literally what Congress argues about every single year. You just did what they do — except they argue about it for months. And it's trillions of dollars, not a hundred."
Part B

"Reading Your Paycheck" — Ages 13+, 15–20 Minutes

Ages 6–12

Younger kids are done after Part A. Give them free time, a related coloring activity, or have them refine their budget allocations while you work with the older kids.

1

Introduce the Pay Stub (3 minutes)

Hand out the Mock Pay Stub.

"Remember in Build Your Life when you saw gross pay versus net pay? You saw that $4,000 turned into $3,080 — and some of you were pretty shocked. Now we're going to look at a REAL pay stub and see EXACTLY where that money went. Every. Single. Dollar."
2

Walk Through Each Line (5 minutes)

Go line by line through the pay stub. Explain each deduction:

  • Federal Income Tax ($186): "This goes to the federal government — all those categories we just talked about in Part A. Healthcare, military, Social Security, roads — all of it."
  • State Income Tax ($0): "Jordan lives in Florida, which has NO state income tax. Only some states charge this. If Jordan lived in California, this line would NOT be zero."
  • Social Security — 6.2% ($124): "This is FICA. It stands for Federal Insurance Contributions Act. This money goes into a fund that pays retired people a monthly check. The idea is: you pay in now, and when you're 67, you get paid back."
  • Medicare — 1.45% ($29): "Also part of FICA. This pays for healthcare for people over 65."
  • Health Insurance ($74): "This isn't a tax — it's Jordan's share of health insurance through work. The company pays part, Jordan pays part."
  • 401(k) — 3% ($60): "This is ALSO not a tax. This is Jordan choosing to save for retirement. And here's the cool part — many companies MATCH what you put in. So if Jordan puts in $60, the company might put in another $60. That's free money."
3

The Surprise (2 minutes)

"So Jordan earns $2,000 every two weeks. That sounds great, right? But look at the net pay: $1,527. That's $473 gone. Almost a quarter of the paycheck. And over a full year? Jordan earns $52,000 but takes home roughly $39,700. More than $12,000 goes to deductions."

Let them react. This is usually a genuine surprise moment.

"But here's the thing — not all of it is 'lost.' The 401(k) is going to YOUR retirement. The health insurance is protecting YOU. It's really only the taxes that go to the government. And even those pay for things you use every day."
4

The Worksheet (8–10 minutes)

Hand out the Pay Stub Worksheet. Let them work through it. This is where the math happens.

Facilitator Tip

Let them use calculators for the percentage and annual calculations. The goal is understanding, not mental math. Walk through the verification problems together if they struggle.

5

W-2 Preview — Age 15 Only (2 minutes)

"One more thing for you. When you get a job — any job, even a part-time one — every January your employer will send you something called a W-2. It shows exactly how much you earned that year and how much was taken out for taxes. You use that W-2 to file your tax return — which is basically telling the government: 'Here's what I earned, here's what I already paid, do I owe more or do you owe ME a refund?' We'll cover filing in more detail later, but just know — the W-2 is coming."

Wrap-Up & Connection to Build Your Life

Build Your Life Connection

In the Build Your Life simulation, kids saw that a $4,000/month salary turned into $3,080 take-home pay. At the time, that gap was just a number. Now they understand exactly what fills that gap — federal tax, state tax, Social Security, Medicare, health insurance, and retirement savings. The next time they see gross vs. net in Build Your Life or any future exercise, it won't be a mystery.

Close with this:

"Taxes are one of those things that everyone has opinions about, but not everyone understands. Now you do. You know where the money goes. You know what a pay stub looks like. And you've made the same impossible trade-offs that Congress makes every year. Whether you end up thinking taxes should be higher, lower, or about the same — at least you'll know what you're talking about."

Quick Reference: Pay Stub Worksheet Answers

For facilitator use during Part B.

  • Hourly rate: $25.00
  • Hours worked: 80
  • Gross pay: $2,000.00
  • Net pay: $1,527.00
  • Total deducted: $473.00
  • Deduction percentage: 23.65%
  • Social Security check: $2,000 x 0.062 = $124.00
  • Medicare check: $2,000 x 0.0145 = $29.00
  • Annual gross: $25 x 40 x 52 = $52,000
  • Annual take-home (approx): $1,527 x 26 = $39,702
  • Annual deductions: $52,000 - $39,702 = $12,298
  • State tax is $0 because Florida has no state income tax
  • 401(k) is different because it goes to the employee's own retirement savings, not to the government
