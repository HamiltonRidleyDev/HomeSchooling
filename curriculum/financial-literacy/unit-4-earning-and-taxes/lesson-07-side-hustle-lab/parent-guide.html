The Side Hustle Lab

Parent Guide & Facilitator Notes

What is this? In Build Your Life (Lesson 1), every kid pitched a side hustle on paper. Now they pick one and ACTUALLY DO IT. The jump from hypothetical to real is where entrepreneurship lives. They will discover that reality is harder than paper—but also more rewarding. This is a real execution lesson: kids run an actual business for 1–2 weeks.

Structure: 3 touchpoints over 1–2 weeks
Touchpoint 1: Planning Session (20 minutes, all together)
Touchpoint 2: 1–2 weeks of real execution (kids work independently with parent check-ins)
Touchpoint 3: Debrief Session (20 minutes, all together)

Players: 6 kids, ages 6–15
Materials: Business Plan worksheets (1 per kid), Daily Income/Expense Logs (1 per kid), Debrief worksheets (1 per kid), pencils, any supplies the kids need for their businesses
Prep time: 5 minutes to print worksheets. Pull out Lesson 1 side hustle pitches if you saved them.
Connection to Lesson 1 (Build Your Life): In the budget simulation, every kid pitched a side hustle as part of building their financial life. Those pitches were theoretical. This lesson makes them real. When kids hit the gap between "I thought I'd earn $50" and "I actually earned $12," they learn something no worksheet can teach: the difference between a plan and execution. That gap is where real financial literacy lives.

Touchpoint 1: Planning Session (20 min)

The goal is simple: every kid leaves this session with a concrete, approved plan they can start executing tomorrow.

1
Review the Pitches (5 min)

Pull out the side hustle ideas from Build Your Life. Let each kid remind the group what they pitched. If you don't have the old worksheets, ask: "What business idea did you come up with in Lesson 1?"

Let the energy build. "Now here's the thing—that was on paper. This time, you're actually going to DO it."

2
Pick an Idea (3 min)

Each kid picks one business to try. It can be their original pitch or something new. The key criteria:

  • Can you realistically do this in 1–2 weeks?
  • Do you have (or can you get) what you need to start?
  • Is there someone who would actually pay for this?

If a kid is stuck, steer them toward the idea list below.

3
Fill Out the Business Plan (10 min)

Hand out the Business Plan worksheet (age-appropriate version). Walk through it together, but let kids fill in their own answers. Help them think through pricing—see the pricing guide below.

For younger kids (age 6), sit with them and help them draw/write their plan. For older kids, let them work independently and check in.

4
Parent Approval & Launch (2 min)

Review each plan. Make sure it is safe, realistic, and achievable. Sign the approval line. Hand out the Daily Log and explain: "Every day you work, fill in one row. Track every dollar in and every dollar out."

Set a clear timeline: "You have [1 week / 2 weeks] starting tomorrow. We'll do our debrief on [date]."

How to Help Kids Price Their Services

Pricing is one of the hardest parts. Here is a simple framework to walk them through:

1
Cost-Plus Method (simplest)

"What does it cost you to make or do this? Add some on top for your time." If cookies cost $3 in ingredients and take 1 hour, charging $1/cookie for a batch of 12 means $12 revenue, $3 cost, $9 profit, $9/hour.

2
Market Rate Method

"What do other people charge for this?" If a car wash place charges $15, a kid charging $8 for a driveway wash is a deal. For lawn mowing, check what local services charge and price below that.

3
The "Would You Pay That?" Test

Ask the kid: "If someone offered you this service at this price, would YOU pay for it?" If the answer is no, the price is too high or the value isn't clear enough.

Common pricing mistakes: Kids tend to underprice. Encourage them to value their time. If a 13-year-old spends 2 hours mowing a lawn for $5, that is $2.50/hour. Help them see that $15–20 is reasonable for that work. On the flip side, a 6-year-old selling a single drawing for $20 may need a gentle reality check.

Touchpoint 2: Execution Period (1–2 Weeks)

This is where the real learning happens. Your job as parent is to step back and let them work—but stay close enough to support when needed.

Your Role During Execution

  • Safety monitor: Make sure all activities are safe and supervised as needed (see safety rules below).
  • Logistics helper: Drive them to customers, help buy supplies, provide workspace. You are support staff, not the CEO.
  • Daily log reminder: "Did you fill in your log today?" Every day they work, they should track income and expenses.
  • Encourager, not rescuer: When things go wrong (and they will), resist the urge to fix it. Ask questions instead.

Check-In Conversations

Have a brief conversation every few days. Keep it casual—dinner table is perfect. Use these prompts:

"How's it going? What did you do today?"
"What's been harder than you expected?"
"What's working well? What are you proud of?"
"Have you found any customers? How did you find them?"
"Are you on track to hit your goal? What would you need to change?"
"Is there anything you need help with?"
The magic question: "What would you do differently if you started over tomorrow?" This question builds entrepreneurial thinking even when the business isn't going well.

Safety Guidelines

Non-negotiable safety rules (review these with every child before they start):
  • Never go to a stranger's home alone. All services at unfamiliar locations require a parent present or a parent-approved adult nearby.
  • Parent must approve all activities and customers. No new customers without checking with Mom or Dad first.
  • Younger kids (under 10) work only at home, at neighbors' homes you know well, or in the front yard/driveway with a parent nearby.
  • No door-to-door sales to unknown houses. Stick to people you know: family, friends, neighbors, church community, etc.
  • Online sales: Parent handles all online interactions. Kid makes the product or service; parent manages the listing, communication, and shipping.
  • Food safety: If selling food, follow basic hygiene (hand washing, clean workspace, proper storage). Label any common allergens.
  • Money handling: Keep all money in a safe place at home. Younger kids should give earnings to a parent for safekeeping daily.

When Things Don't Go As Planned

Some kids will earn money. Some won't. Both outcomes are valuable. Here is how to handle common struggles:

SituationWhat to SayWhy It Matters
"Nobody wants to buy my stuff." "That's real information. Why do you think that is? Is it the product, the price, or that people don't know about it?" Marketing and product-market fit are real business skills.
"This is harder than I thought." "You're right—it IS hard. Every business owner says that. What's the hardest part? Can we solve just that one thing?" Resilience. Breaking big problems into small ones.
"I want to quit." "I hear you. Let's talk about it at the debrief. You've already learned a ton—quitting after trying is different from never trying." Not every business works. Learning what DOESN'T work is also valuable. Praise the effort, not just the income.
"I only made $3." "$3 more than you had! And now you know how to make $3. How would you turn that into $30?" Scaling thinking. Small wins are still wins.
Kid is crushing it and wants to do more "Amazing! What if you had two customers? Five? What would need to change?" Growth mindset. Systems thinking.
Praise the effort regardless of income. A kid who tried for two weeks and earned $0 learned more about business than a kid who never tried. Frame this as: "You now know something most adults never learn—what it actually takes to earn money on your own."

Touchpoint 3: Debrief Session (20 min)

Gather everyone together. This is a celebration of effort, a presentation of results, and a discussion about what they learned.

1
Fill Out the Debrief Worksheet (5 min)

Hand out the Debrief worksheets. Let kids transfer their totals from the Daily Log and fill in the reflection questions. Help younger kids with the math.

2
Presentations (10 min, ~90 seconds each)

Each kid presents their results to the family. They should cover: what they did, how much they earned, what they learned, and their 30-second pitch. Encourage the audience to ask one question after each presentation.

Go youngest to oldest. Celebrate every presenter—clapping, high-fives, the works.

3
Family Discussion (5 min)

Open it up for group discussion. Key questions to ask:

  • "What surprised you most about actually running a business?"
  • "What was the difference between your plan and what actually happened?"
  • "Would you keep doing this? How would you grow it?"
  • "What would you tell someone who is about to start their first side hustle?"

End with: "You just did something most people only talk about. You took an idea and made it real. That takes guts. Whether you made $100 or $1 or $0, you are now someone who has started a business. That's a big deal."

Age-Tier Adjustments

Primary (Age 6) Simplified Experience

  • Business plan is simplified: "What will I do? Who will I help? What do I need?" with drawing space.
  • Daily log may need parent help to fill in. Focus on the experience, not the bookkeeping.
  • Debrief is "What did I do? Did I earn money? What was fun? What was hard? Draw a picture."
  • Parent should be hands-on with this age—help set up the stand, make the product together, handle the money. The kid's job is the "doing" and the "selling."
  • Keep it to 1 week max. Attention spans are short at this age.

Elementary (Ages 9–10) Guided Experience

  • Full business plan with guided fields. Help with pricing and customer identification.
  • Daily log: they should be able to fill this in themselves with a nightly reminder.
  • Can work semi-independently but need parent nearby for safety and logistics.
  • Debrief covers results, reflection, and the "would you keep going?" question.

Middle School (Ages 11–13) Independent with Coaching

  • Business plan adds: pricing strategy, competitive analysis, marketing plan.
  • Should manage their own Daily Log without reminders.
  • Can execute mostly independently. Parent provides transportation and safety oversight.
  • Debrief includes hourly rate calculations and growth analysis.

High School (Age 15) Full Entrepreneur Mode

  • Business plan includes full competitive analysis, marketing strategy, and pricing justification.
  • Fully independent execution. Parent is available if needed but not hovering.
  • Debrief includes comparison to career card hourly rates from Lesson 1, scaling analysis, and annual projection.
  • Challenge them: "Could this become a real ongoing business? What would it take?"
Looking Ahead: The income kids earn in this lesson feeds directly into Lesson 8 (Taxes). When they learn about taxes, they will have REAL income numbers to work with. "Remember the $47 you earned mowing lawns? Let's talk about what the government would take..." This makes taxes concrete, not abstract.
