The 5 Factors That Determine Your Score

35% Payment History
30% Credit Utilization
15% Length of Credit History
10% Credit Mix
10% New Credit Inquiries
1. Payment History 35%
The single biggest factor. Do you pay your bills on time?
"ONE late payment can drop your score 100 points. ONE. It stays on your record for 7 years. Set up autopay and never miss a due date."
2. Credit Utilization 30%
How much of your available credit are you using?
"If your credit limit is $1,000, using more than $300 hurts your score. Using less than $100 is best. Think of it as a test: the less you need to borrow, the more they trust you."
3. Length of Credit History 15%
How long have you had credit accounts?
"Older accounts = better score. This is why starting young gives you a superpower. A 16-year-old who becomes an authorized user will have 5+ years of history by age 21."
4. Credit Mix 10%
Do you have different types of credit?
"Having a credit card + a student loan + a car loan (all in good standing) shows you can handle different kinds of borrowing. Don't open accounts just for this — it happens naturally over time."
5. New Credit Inquiries 10%
Have you applied for lots of new credit recently?
"Applying for 4 credit cards in one month looks desperate. Each application (called a 'hard inquiry') can ding your score. Space out applications. Note: checking your OWN score is a 'soft inquiry' and does NOT affect it."

Who Cares About Your Credit Score?

More people than you think check this number before doing business with you:

🏠
Landlords Will they rent you an apartment? Low score = denied or extra deposit required.
🏦
Banks & Lenders Will they give you a loan? At what interest rate? This is where the BIG money difference happens.
🚗
Car Dealers What interest rate do you get on an auto loan? Bad credit = thousands more for the same car.
📋
Insurance Companies Some states allow insurers to use credit scores when setting your rates. Lower score = higher premiums.
💼
Some Employers Especially in finance, government, and security jobs. A credit check can be part of the hiring process.
📱
Cell Phone Companies Good credit = phone lease or installment plan. Bad credit = pay full price upfront.

How to Build Credit (Starting Young)

Ages 13–17

Become an Authorized User

A parent adds you to one of their credit cards with a long, positive history. You don't even need to use the card. Their payment history starts building YOUR credit file. Make sure the card issuer reports authorized users to the credit bureaus.

Age 18+

Get a Secured Credit Card

You deposit $200–$500 at a bank. That deposit becomes your credit limit. Use it for one small recurring purchase (like a streaming subscription). Pay the full balance every month. After 6–12 months of on-time payments, you can upgrade to a regular card and get your deposit back.

Age 18+

Student Credit Card or Credit-Builder Loan

Some banks offer student cards with small limits. Credit-builder loans deposit your loan amount into savings while you make payments — building history and savings at the same time.

The Golden Rules — At Any Age

  • Pay every bill on time. Every time. No exceptions. This is 35% of your score. Set up autopay.
  • Use less than 30% of your credit limit. Under 10% is even better. If your limit is $1,000, keep balances below $300.
  • Don't close old accounts. Even if you don't use the card much, the length of history matters. Put one small charge on it every few months.
  • Don't open many new accounts at once. Space applications out. Each one creates a hard inquiry.
  • Check your credit report annually. You get a free report from each bureau once a year at AnnualCreditReport.com. Look for errors and dispute them.

Good Debt vs. Bad Debt

Good Debt (Can Help You) Bad Debt (Hurts You)
Mortgage — Builds equity in a home that typically appreciates in value Credit card balance for impulse buys — High interest on things that lose value instantly
Student loans — If for a career that pays well enough to repay them and increase your lifetime earnings Payday loans — Extremely high interest (often 400%+ APR). Designed to trap you in a cycle
Business loan — To start or grow a business that generates income exceeding the loan cost Car loan you can't afford — Cars lose 20% of their value in year one. Borrowing too much = underwater fast
Low-interest debt for appreciating assets — Things that grow in value over time Any high-interest debt for depreciating stuff — Clothes, electronics, vacations on credit
The Test for Any Debt:
"Will this debt help me earn more money or own something that grows in value?"
If YES → it might be good debt. If NO → it's probably bad debt.
