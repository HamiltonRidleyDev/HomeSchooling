Buying a Home

The Biggest Purchase of Your Life
Unit 6: Debt and Big Decisions • Lesson 12 Reference Sheet B

What Is a Mortgage?

A loan for a house — specifically designed for buying real estate. Much larger than any other loan most people ever take.
15 or 30 years to pay back — most people choose 30 years. That's a long time to make payments every single month.
The bank technically owns it — until you pay off the loan completely. If you stop paying, the bank can take the house (foreclosure).
You pay interest the entire time — and with a 30-year loan, you end up paying MORE in interest than the house originally cost.
Key Terms: Principal = the amount you borrowed. Interest = what the bank charges you for borrowing. Down Payment = cash you pay upfront (usually 5-20% of the price). Equity = the portion of the home you actually own (home value minus what you still owe).

What is PMI? Private Mortgage Insurance. If you put less than 20% down, the bank makes you pay extra insurance to protect THEM in case you stop paying. It adds $100-$200/month until you build 20% equity. Another reason to save a big down payment.

But Here's the Good News: EQUITY

Why Buying a Home Can Still Be Smart

  • You build ownership. Unlike rent, every mortgage payment increases the portion of the home that's YOURS. This is called equity.
  • Homes historically appreciate 3-5% per year. Your $300,000 home might be worth $400,000 in 10 years and $500,000+ in 15-20 years.
  • You can use your equity. Sell the home and keep the profit, refinance to a better loan, or borrow against it for other investments.
  • When it's paid off, you live there (almost) free. After 30 years, no more mortgage payments. You only pay taxes, insurance, and maintenance — a fraction of the cost.
  • Tax benefits. You can deduct mortgage interest from your taxes, which saves money every year.

"The common wisdom is 'renting is throwing money away.' But buying when you're not ready can be MUCH worse. A house you can't afford leads to stress, debt, and possibly foreclosure."

The right choice depends on YOUR situation. There is no one-size-fits-all answer.

When to Rent vs. When to Buy

Rent When...

  • You might move in less than 5 years
  • You don't have a solid emergency fund + down payment
  • You're in an expensive market where buying costs way more than renting
  • Your job or life situation might change soon
  • You want someone else to handle repairs
  • You're young and still figuring out where to settle

Buy When...

  • You plan to stay at least 5-7 years
  • You have 20% down payment saved (or at least 10%)
  • You have a stable income and good credit
  • You have an emergency fund PLUS repair savings
  • Monthly cost (with ALL expenses) is under 28% of gross income
  • You want to build long-term wealth through equity

The Bottom Line

A home is the biggest purchase most people ever make. The sticker price is just the beginning — interest, taxes, insurance, and maintenance can nearly triple the real cost over 30 years. But unlike almost anything else you buy, a home can grow in value and eventually be yours free and clear. The key is buying only when you're truly ready — financially and personally.
